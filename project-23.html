🟡 PROJECT 23: "I'm Happy That, I'm Afraid That"

You're reacting emotionally to news — joy, fear, surprise, doubt — and the grammar shifts beneath you

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're in France — life is happening around you — people share news constantly
→ Your friend says: "J'ai trouvé un nouveau travail !" (I found a new job!)
→ You want to say: "I'm happy that you found a new job!"
→ You try:

❌ "Je suis content que tu as trouvé un nouveau travail."

→ A French person would say:

✅ "Je suis content que tu AIES trouvé un nouveau travail."

→ That word — aies — is not a typo, not a mistake
→ It's the SUBJONCTIF — a special verb mood that activates when emotions, doubts, wishes, or judgments enter the sentence

→ Your colleague says: "Marie ne peut pas venir à la réunion."
→ You want to say: "It's a shame she can't come."
→ You try:

❌ "C'est dommage qu'elle ne peut pas venir."

→ Correct version:

✅ "C'est dommage qu'elle ne PUISSE pas venir."

→ Puisse — another subjonctif form — replacing the indicatif "peut"

The core realization:
→ In Project 22, you stated opinions — those used indicatif (normal verb forms)
→ "Je pense que c'est vrai" → indicatif — you're stating what you THINK is real
→ But when you EXPRESS EMOTIONS about something — the grammar shifts
→ Emotions don't state reality — they react to it subjectively
→ French marks this shift with a different verb mood: the SUBJONCTIF
→ This project introduces the subjonctif through its most common trigger: emotional reactions

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of French as having two lenses for looking at reality:

LensNameWhen UsedExample
🔍 Objective lensINDICATIFFacts, opinions stated as beliefs, descriptionsJe sais que tu es là.
💭 Subjective lensSUBJONCTIFEmotions, doubts, wishes, necessities, judgmentsJe suis content que tu sois là.

→ The subjonctif is NOT a tense — it's a MOOD
→ It doesn't tell you WHEN something happens — it tells you HOW the speaker FEELS about it
→ The structure is always:

TRIGGER (emotion/doubt/wish/necessity) + QUE + SUBJONCTIF VERB

→ Your job in Project 23: Learn the emotional triggers and the key subjonctif forms
→ Full subjonctif mastery (all triggers, all forms, all exceptions) comes in Project 31
→ This project = your first solid exposure through the most natural context: reacting to life

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

Step 1 — The Subjonctif Forms You Need NOW

→ The subjonctif has its own conjugation — but many forms look identical to the indicatif présent
→ For regular verbs, only nous and vous forms look different
→ For irregular verbs, the forms are completely different — these are the ones to memorize

Regular Subjonctif Formation:

Take the ILS/ELLES form of présent indicatif → remove -ent → add subjonctif endings:

PARLER: ils parlent → parl-
que je parle          que nous parlions
que tu parles         que vous parliez
qu'il/elle parle      qu'ils/elles parlent

FINIR: ils finissent → finiss-
que je finisse        que nous finissions
que tu finisses       que vous finissiez
qu'il/elle finisse    qu'ils/elles finissent

→ For je, tu, il/elle, ils/elles → forms often LOOK like présent indicatif
→ For nous, vous → forms look like IMPARFAIT
→ This is why context (the trigger word) tells you it's subjonctif

⚠️ THE "NE EXPLÉTIF" — A French Peculiarity:

After FEAR verbs (craindre, avoir peur), you may see an EXTRA "ne" 
that does NOT mean negation:

→ Je crains qu'il NE fasse froid.
   = I fear it WILL be cold (not "won't be cold")

→ J'ai peur qu'elle NE vienne pas.
   = I'm afraid she WON'T come (the "pas" makes it negative — the "ne" before was expletive)

This "ne explétif" is:
→ OPTIONAL in modern spoken French
→ More common in WRITTEN / FORMAL French
→ Does NOT change the meaning — it's a stylistic leftover
→ For now: IGNORE it in speaking — RECOGNIZE it in reading
→ Full explanation in Project 31

🧠 CRITICAL CONTRAST — AFFIRMATIVE vs. NEGATIVE:

AFFIRMATIVE (certainty → INDICATIF):
✅ Je suis sûr que c'EST vrai.          (indicatif)
✅ Je pense que tu AS raison.           (indicatif)
✅ Je crois qu'il VIENDRA.              (indicatif)

NEGATIVE (doubt → SUBJONCTIF):
✅ Je ne suis pas sûr que ce SOIT vrai.  (subjonctif)
✅ Je ne pense pas que tu AIES raison.   (subjonctif)
✅ Je ne crois pas qu'il VIENNE.         (subjonctif)

WHY? → Affirming = you present it as REAL → indicatif
     → Denying = you introduce DOUBT → subjonctif
     → The NEGATION flips the mood

🧠 CRITICAL INSIGHT — WHY DESIRE TRIGGERS SUBJONCTIF:

When you WANT something → it hasn't happened yet → it's NOT reality
→ You're expressing a WISH about someone else's action
→ The subjonctif marks this as "desired but not certain"

COMPARE:
→ Je sais que tu ES honnête.     (fact → indicatif)
→ Je veux que tu SOIS honnête.   (wish → subjonctif)

The same information — "tu / honnête" — but the LENS changes:
→ SAIS = I know it's real → indicatif
→ VEUX = I want it to be real → subjonctif

🧠 IMPORTANT — "Vouloir" with Same Subject:

When the subject is THE SAME for both verbs → use INFINITIF, NOT subjonctif:

TWO DIFFERENT subjects:
✅ Je veux que TU viennes.     (I want YOU to come → subjonctif)

SAME subject:
✅ Je veux venir.              (I want to come → infinitif)
❌ Je veux que je vienne.      (WRONG — never subjonctif with same subject)

This rule applies to ALL subjonctif triggers:
→ Je suis content de venir.        (same subject → de + infinitif)
→ Je suis content que tu viennes.   (different subject → que + subjonctif)

⚠️ CRITICAL EXCEPTION — SENTENCE 73 — "ESPÉRER QUE":

"ESPÉRER QUE" (to hope that) does NOT take subjonctif
→ It takes INDICATIF

❌ J'espère qu'il VIENNE.        (WRONG)
✅ J'espère qu'il VIENDRA.       (CORRECT — futur indicatif)
✅ J'espère que ça IRA.          (CORRECT — futur indicatif)
✅ J'espère que tu VAS bien.     (CORRECT — présent indicatif)

WHY? → "Espérer" presents hope as a POSITIVE EXPECTATION — almost a belief
→ French treats it as closer to "croire" than to "vouloir"
→ This is THE MOST COMMON exception — memorize it now
→ Every other "wish/hope" verb (souhaiter, vouloir, aimer) → subjonctif
→ ESPÉRER alone → indicatif

🧠 THE SAME-SUBJECT RULE — FORMALIZED:

TWO DIFFERENT SUBJECTS → QUE + SUBJONCTIF:
Je veux    que TU viennes.
   ↑ je         ↑ tu (different) → subjonctif

SAME SUBJECT → DE + INFINITIF (or just INFINITIF):
Je veux    venir.
   ↑ je   ↑ je (same) → infinitif

EMOTIONAL ADJECTIVES use "de" before infinitif:
→ content DE + infinitif
→ triste DE + infinitif  
→ surpris DE + infinitif
→ fier DE + infinitif
→ heureux DE + infinitif

VERBS use direct infinitif:
→ vouloir + infinitif (no "de")
→ souhaiter + infinitif (no "de")
→ préférer + infinitif (no "de")

FEAR uses "de" before infinitif:
→ avoir peur DE + infinitif
→ craindre DE + infinitif

→ This rule is non-negotiable — you CANNOT use subjonctif when both subjects are the same
→ ❌ Je veux que je vienne → ✅ Je veux venir

🧠 NOTICE THE FLOW — Sentences 96 and 100 use INDICATIF amid subjonctif sentences:
→ 96: "J'espère qu'il trouvera" — espérer = indicatif (the famous exception)
→ 100: "Je suis sûre que ça ira" — être sûr(e) (affirmative) = indicatif (certainty)
→ In real conversation, indicatif and subjonctif alternate based on the trigger
→ Your brain must learn to switch automatically based on the trigger word

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Memorize the 10 irregular subjonctif verbs — especially être (sois/soit/soient) and avoir (aie/aies/ait/aient)
→ Start with "C'est dommage que" and "Il faut que" — these are the most natural entry points
→ Always check: same subject or different? → Same = infinitif / Different = subjonctif
→ Practice converting indicatif to subjonctif — "tu es" → "que tu sois" / "il a" → "qu'il ait"
→ Listen for subjonctif in real French — you'll start hearing "soit", "ait", "fasse", "puisse" everywhere
→ Memorize the espérer exception — it's the trap that catches everyone

→ But what about when things are done TO something — not BY someone?
→ "The house was built in 1900" / "The decision was made by the director"
→ This requires the passive voice — a completely different sentence architecture

🌉 BRIDGE TO PROJECT 24

You can now express opinions (Project 22) and emotional reactions (Project 23)
Both are active — YOU think, YOU feel, YOU want

But sometimes the focus is on WHAT HAPPENED TO SOMETHING — not who did it:

→ "Ce restaurant a été recommandé par un ami." (This restaurant was recommended by a friend.)
→ "La décision a été prise hier." (The decision was made yesterday.)
→ "Le bâtiment sera construit l'année prochaine." (The building will be built next year.)

→ The passive voice flips the sentence — the object becomes the subject
→ It's used in news, formal speech, descriptions, and storytelling
→ French has specific rules about when passive works and when it doesn't
