🟢 PROJECT 2: "My Day in 20 Verbs"

You need to describe your daily routine to a French host family

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're staying with a French host family in Lyon for one month
→ On your first evening, the family asks: "Alors, c'est quoi ta journée typique ?" (So, what's your typical day like?)
→ You already know how to say who you are and what you have (Project 1)
→ But now you need to describe what you DO — eating, working, speaking, going, coming, sleeping
→ You need action engines — verbs that describe movement, activity, routine
→ French has 3 main verb families + key irregular verbs that don't follow rules
→ If you don't know which family a verb belongs to → you will conjugate it wrong → your sentence breaks

The core realization:
→ Project 1 gave you 2 engines (être, avoir) for static states
→ Project 2 gives you 20 new engines for actions
→ These 20 verbs cover approximately 70% of daily spoken French

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ French verbs are grouped by their ending in the dictionary form (infinitif)
→ The ending tells you which conjugation pattern to follow

Verb FamilyEndingExample% of French Verbs
Family 1-ERParler (to speak)~90% of all verbs
Family 2-IRFinir (to finish)~7%
Family 3-REAttendre (to wait)~3%
RebelsVariousAller, Faire, VenirMust memorize individually

→ Your approach: Master the -ER pattern first (it covers almost everything)
→ Then learn the -IR and -RE patterns
→ Then handle the rebels (irregulars) one by one

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

Step 1 — The -ER Conjugation Engine

Rule: Remove -er → Add these endings

SubjectEndingExample: PARLER
Je-eje parle
Tu-estu parles
Il / Elle / On-eil parle
Nous-onsnous parlons
Vous-ezvous parlez
Ils / Elles-entils parlent

🔊 Pronunciation secret: je parle, tu parles, il parle, ils parlent → ALL SOUND THE SAME ("parl")
→ Only nous parlons and vous parlez sound different
→ This means in spoken French, you MUST hear the subject to know who is speaking

Step 2 — The -IR Conjugation Engine

Rule: Remove -ir → Add these endings

SubjectEndingExample: FINIR
Je-isje finis
Tu-istu finis
Il / Elle / On-itil finit
Nous-issonsnous finissons
Vous-issezvous finissez
Ils / Elles-issentils finissent

Step 3 — The -RE Conjugation Engine

Rule: Remove -re → Add these endings

SubjectEndingExample: ATTENDRE
Je-sj'attends
Tu-stu attends
Il / Elle / On— (nothing)il attend
Nous-onsnous attendons
Vous-ezvous attendez
Ils / Elles-entils attendent

🔹 BLOCK C — -ER Verbs with Spelling Quirks

Scenario: Shopping, preparing meals, starting your day

#French SentenceEnglish MeaningSpelling Note

🧠 Spelling rule unlocked:
→ Acheter type → è appears when the ending is silent (je, tu, il, ils forms)
→ Commencer type → ç appears before -ons to keep the soft "s" sound
→ These are NOT irregular — they are regular verbs with spelling adjustments

🧠 Critical distinction unlocked:
→ Finir / Choisir type = add -iss- in plural → nous finissons
→ Dormir / Partir / Sortir type = NO -iss- → nous dormons, nous partons, nous sortons
→ Singular forms lose the last consonant of the stem: dormir → je dors (the "m" disappears!)

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Learn the -ER pattern COLD — it covers 90% of verbs you'll ever meet
→ Memorize the rebel -IR verbs (dormir, partir, sortir) as a separate group from regular -IR (finir, choisir)
→ Memorize ALLER, FAIRE, VENIR completely — they appear in almost every conversation
→ Practice building sentences in this order: Subject → Verb → Object → Time/Place
→ Use "on" instead of "nous" in casual speech (as mentioned in Project 1)

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT apply the -ER pattern to -IR or -RE verbs
→ ❌ Do NOT add -iss- to dormir/partir/sortir → nous dormissons → nous dormons
→ ❌ Do NOT conjugate aller like a regular -ER verb → je alle → je vais
→ ❌ Do NOT forget that ils parlent is SILENT on the "-ent" → pronounced "parl" not "parlent"
→ ❌ Do NOT say je mange à le restaurant → au restaurant (à + le = au, from Project 1)
→ ❌ Do NOT confuse habiter (to live/reside) with vivre (to live/exist) — at this level use habiter for "where you live"

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Do NOT use aller to mean "to be doing well" in a sentence about movement → context matters
→ → "Je vais bien" = I'm doing well (état)
→ → "Je vais au bureau" = I'm going to the office (movement)
→ Do NOT use faire when a specific verb exists
→ → Je fais de la cuisine is acceptable BUT je cuisine is more natural for "I cook"
→ → Faire is for when the activity is expressed as a noun phrase: faire la cuisine, faire les courses, faire du sport
→ Do NOT use present tense for completed past events (that's Project 9)
→ Do NOT use present tense for distant future plans (that's Project 12 & 16)

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

Project 1 gave you the ability to EXIST (être) and POSSESS (avoir).
Project 2 gives you the ability to ACT — to describe everything you DO in a day.

The sentence skeleton has expanded:

  • Project 1: Subject + ÊTRE/AVOIR + information
  • Project 2: Subject + ACTION VERB + object + time/place/manner

With just être + avoir + 20 action verbs, you can now describe:
→ Who you are → What you have → What you do → Where you go → When you do it

But you can only describe your WORLD — you cannot yet point at THE world.
→ "This is good" / "There are three" / "It's a restaurant" — you can't say these yet.
