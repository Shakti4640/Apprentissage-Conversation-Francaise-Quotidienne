🟠 PROJECT 39: "Never, Nothing, Nobody, Only"

A detective investigating a case where everyone denies everything

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You are a detective working on a case in Marseille
→ A valuable painting has disappeared from a private gallery
→ You interrogate witnesses, suspects, staff — and EVERYONE denies EVERYTHING
→ "I never went there." "I saw nothing." "Nobody told me anything." "I have no idea."
→ "I spoke to neither the owner nor the guard." "I only came to see the exhibition."
→ "I no longer work there." "Not one person saw anything." "None of the cameras were working."
→ You try to write your report in French using only "ne...pas" for every negation
→ Your French colleague reads it and says: "This sounds like a child wrote it — there are TEN ways to say no in French, and you're using only one."
→ You realize: P5 gave you ne...pas — the basic "not"
→ But real French negation is a rich system with at least 8 distinct negative structures
→ Each one has different placement rules, different interactions with tenses, and different emotional weight
→ Without them, you can deny — but you can't deny with precision, emphasis, or nuance

The core realization:
→ P5 taught you: ne...pas = not (the default negation)
→ P39 teaches you the ENTIRE negative system — every way French says "no"
→ These negations interact with EVERY structure you've learned: compound tenses, pronouns, infinitives, gérondif, faire causatif, si-clauses
→ Mastering negation = mastering the ability to set boundaries, deny accusations, express absence, and be precise

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of French negation as a sandwich — NE is always the first slice, and the second slice changes meaning

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

The Universal Placement Rule for Simple Tenses

SUBJECT + NE + VERB + [second element]

→ Je ne mange pas / plus / jamais / rien / que du pain

The Placement Rule for Compound Tenses — HERE'S WHERE IT SPLITS

Group 1 — Second element goes BETWEEN auxiliary and past participle (same position as "pas")

NegationCompound Tense PlacementExample
ne...pasne + aux + pas + ppJe n'ai pas mangé
ne...plusne + aux + plus + ppJe n'ai plus mangé
ne...jamaisne + aux + jamais + ppJe n'ai jamais mangé
ne...rienne + aux + rien + ppJe n'ai rien mangé

Group 2 — Second element goes AFTER the past participle

NegationCompound Tense PlacementExample
ne...personnene + aux + pp + personneJe n'ai vu personne
ne...quene + aux + pp + que + nounJe n'ai mangé que du pain
ne...aucun(e)ne + aux + pp + aucun(e) + nounJe n'ai trouvé aucune preuve
ne...nulle partne + aux + pp + nulle partJe ne suis allé nulle part

🧠 The critical split:
→ Short words (pas, plus, jamais, rien) → go BEFORE past participle
→ Long words / phrases (personne, que+noun, aucun+noun, nulle part) → go AFTER past participle
→ This is THE most important rule in advanced negation

🧠 Structure unlocked: NE + verb + QUE + [the only thing] = only / nothing but

Critical placement rule: QUE goes directly BEFORE the element being limited

Je ne mange QUE du pain.     (I only eat bread — bread is the limited element)
Je n'ai mangé QUE du pain.   (I only ate bread — que AFTER pp, before limited element)

→ QUE is NOT a true negation — it's a restriction
→ It doesn't negate the action — it limits WHAT the action applies to
→ Sentence 46: "Tu n'as qu'à demander" = You just have to ask — extremely common fixed expression
→ Sentence 49: "Ce **n'est qu'**une hypothèse" = It's only a hypothesis — another very common pattern

→ NE...QUE vs. SEULEMENT:

  • "Je ne mange que du pain" = "Je mange seulement du pain" — same meaning
  • NE...QUE is more elegant / seulement is more casual
  • Both are correct — ne...que is more "French-sounding"

🧠 Structure unlocked: NE + verb + NI + element + NI + element = neither...nor

Critical rules:

→ Articles DROP after NI (in most cases):

  • ✅ Il ne boit ni Ø café ni Ø thé (no articles)

→ NI...NI as subject: Both elements come before NE → "Ni lui ni moi ne savons"
→ Verb agreement with ni...ni subject: traditionally singular (the idea is "not one, not the other"), but plural is increasingly accepted in spoken French
→ Sentence 63: NI can separate two past participles → "n'a ni confirmé ni nié"
→ Sentence 70: NI can separate two infinitives → "ne veut ni parler ni coopérer"

ce n'est plus qu'une question de temps
         │      │
      ne...plus  ne...que
      (no longer)  (only)

→ "Ne...plus...que" = "now only" — combining "no longer" + "only"

→ The negations don't cancel each other — they STACK meaning

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Memorize the two groups — this is the foundation of all placement:

  • Before pp: pas, plus, jamais, rien (short words)
  • After pp: personne, que+noun, aucun+noun, nulle part (long elements)

→ Practice replacing PAS with each negation one at a time:

  • Je n'ai pas mangé → Je n'ai plus mangé → Je n'ai jamais mangé → Je n'ai rien mangé
  • Je n'ai vu pas → Je n'ai vu personne → Je n'ai trouvé aucune preuve

→ Master subject-position negations:

  • Rien ne... / Personne ne... / Aucun(e) [noun] ne... / Ni...ni ne...
  • These START the sentence and take NE after them — no "pas" anywhere

→ Practice stacking: jamais + rien, plus + personne, jamais + rien + personne
→ Remember: PAS never combines with other negation words — it's replaced by them
→ Connect to P5 — basic ne...pas → now you have the FULL system
→ Connect to P36 — double pronouns interact with negation: "Je ne le lui ai jamais dit"
→ Connect to P38 — gérondif negation: "En ne disant rien..."

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ NEVER combine PAS with another negation word:

  • ❌ Je n'ai pas jamais vu → ✅ Je n'ai jamais vu
  • ❌ Je n'ai pas rien dit → ✅ Je n'ai rien dit
  • ❌ Je n'ai pas vu personne → ✅ Je n'ai vu personne
  • PAS disappears when replaced by a more specific negation

→ ❌ NEVER put PERSONNE before the past participle:

  • ❌ Je n'ai personne vu → ✅ Je n'ai vu personne
  • Personne ALWAYS goes AFTER the pp (unlike rien which goes before)

→ ❌ NEVER put RIEN after the past participle:

  • ❌ Je n'ai vu rien → ✅ Je n'ai rien vu
  • Rien ALWAYS goes BEFORE the pp (unlike personne which goes after)

→ ❌ NEVER forget to drop articles after NI:

  • ❌ Il ne boit ni du café ni du thé → ✅ Il ne boit ni café ni thé
  • Articles disappear after ni (except definite articles for specific items)

→ ❌ NEVER make AUCUN plural:

  • ❌ aucuns témoins → ✅ aucun témoin (always singular)
  • The whole point of aucun is "not a SINGLE one"

→ ❌ NEVER forget NE in written French (even though spoken French drops it):

  • Written: Je **n'**ai rien vu ✅
  • Spoken: J'ai rien vu ✅ (casual — NE dropped — discussed in P47)
  • But in your report, exam, email → ALWAYS include NE

→ ❌ NEVER confuse QUE (only) with QUE (that/which):

  • "Je ne mange que du pain" = I only eat bread (restriction — ne...que)
  • "Je sais que tu manges du pain" = I know that you eat bread (conjunction — P22)
  • The NE tells you it's the restriction meaning

→ When you want a POSITIVE statement — don't accidentally negate

  • "Quelqu'un" (someone) is the positive of "personne" (nobody)
  • "Quelque chose" (something) is the positive of "rien" (nothing)
  • "Toujours" (always) is the positive of "jamais" (never)
  • "Encore" (still) is the positive of "plus" (no longer)
  • "Partout" (everywhere) is the positive of "nulle part" (nowhere)

→ When NE...QUE would sound too literary → use "seulement" instead

  • "Je n'ai que cinq minutes" (elegant) = "J'ai seulement cinq minutes" (casual)

→ When NE...GUÈRE would sound too old-fashioned → use "ne...pas vraiment" or "à peine"

  • "Je ne le connais guère" (literary) = "Je ne le connais pas vraiment" (modern)

→ When stacking would create confusion → simplify

  • "Il n'a jamais rien dit à personne nulle part" — technically possible but overwhelming
  • Better: split into two sentences for clarity

The two-group placement system is everything:

  • Long elements AFTER past participle: personne, que, aucun, nulle part
