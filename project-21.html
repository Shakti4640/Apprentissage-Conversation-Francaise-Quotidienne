🟡 PROJECT 21: "The Person Who, The Thing That"

You need to describe someone or something using a sub-sentence inside your main sentence

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're at a dinner party in France — people are telling stories, describing friends, recommending places
→ You listen carefully — their sentences are long, elegant, layered
→ They say things like:

"Le restaurant où on a mangé hier soir était excellent."
"C'est l'ami dont je t'ai parlé."
"La fille qui travaille avec moi est très sympa."
"Le film que j'ai vu ce week-end m'a beaucoup plu."

→ You try to say the same things — but you can only build short, choppy sentences:

❌ J'ai un ami. Il habite à Paris. Il parle trois langues. Il est très drôle.
❌ J'ai vu un film. Le film était génial. Mon ami m'a recommandé le film.
❌ Je connais un restaurant. Le restaurant est à Lyon. On mange bien dans ce restaurant.

→ You sound like a list reader, not a storyteller
→ French adults NEVER speak in disconnected short sentences
→ They weave ideas together using four magic connecting words:

WordRole
QuiThe person/thing WHO/THAT does something
QueThe person/thing THAT someone does something to
OùThe place/time WHERE/WHEN something happens
DontThe person/thing OF WHICH / ABOUT WHICH / WHOSE

→ These are relative pronouns — they create a sentence inside a sentence
→ Without them, you speak at a child's level
→ With them, you speak at an adult's level

The core realization:
→ Projects 19-20 taught you to compress nouns into tiny pronouns
→ Project 21 teaches you to connect entire ideas into one flowing sentence
→ This is not just grammar — this is how French people think
→ They don't think in isolated facts — they think in connected descriptions

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Every relative pronoun creates a main clause and a relative clause:

MAIN CLAUSE:      J'ai un ami
RELATIVE CLAUSE:  qui habite à Paris
COMBINED:         J'ai un ami QUI habite à Paris.

→ The antecedent = the noun being described (un ami)
→ The relative pronoun = the connector (qui)
→ The relative clause = the description inserted into the sentence (habite à Paris)

→ Your job: Choose the RIGHT connector based on the role the antecedent plays in the relative clause

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

THE FOUR RELATIVE PRONOUNS — Decision Framework

PronounWhen to UseThe Antecedent Is...English Equivalent
QUIAntecedent = SUBJECT of relative clausedoing the action in the sub-sentencewho / that / which (subject)
QUEAntecedent = DIRECT OBJECT of relative clausereceiving the action in the sub-sentencewhom / that / which (object)
OÙAntecedent = PLACE or TIMEthe location or moment of the sub-sentencewhere / when
DONTAntecedent = linked by DEconnected to a "de" expression in the sub-sentenceof which / about which / whose

THE QUICK TEST — How to Choose

Step 1: Look at the relative clause (the description part)
Step 2: Ask: What role does the described noun play in that clause?

→ It DOES the action?              → QUI
→ Someone does something TO it?    → QUE
→ Something happens IN/AT it?      → OÙ
→ Someone talks ABOUT it / it belongs to someone? → DONT

CRITICAL GRAMMAR NOTES BEFORE SENTENCES

QUI:
→ NEVER contracts before a vowel → "qui a", "qui est" (NOT qu'i)
→ Verb after QUI agrees with the ANTECEDENT
   → C'est moi qui SUIS... (not "qui est")
   → C'est toi qui AS... (not "qui a")

QUE:
→ CONTRACTS before a vowel → QU'
   → Le film qu'elle a vu (NOT "que elle")
→ In passé composé with avoir: past participle AGREES with antecedent
   → La robe que j'ai achetéE (fem agreement — like COD in Project 19)

OÙ:
→ Can mean WHERE or WHEN
→ "Le jour où..." = The day WHEN... (NOT "le jour quand")

DONT:
→ Replaces ALL "de" connections — possession, "parler de", "avoir besoin de", etc.
→ Word order after DONT is ALWAYS: dont + SUBJECT + VERB + (rest)
   → L'homme dont je connais la femme (NOT "dont la femme je connais")

🧠 Structure unlocked: Antecedent + QUE/QU' + subject + verb + rest

→ After QUE, you always see a NEW subject — because the antecedent is NOT the doer
→ QUE contracts to QU' before a vowel: qu'elle, qu'on, qu'il, qu'un

🧠 CRITICAL AGREEMENT RULE — QUE + PASSÉ COMPOSÉ:

When QUE refers to a noun that is the COD of a passé composé with avoir:
→ The past participle AGREES with the antecedent (gender + number)

La robe que j'ai achetéE (fem sing → +e)
Les documents que vous avez demandéS (masc plural → +s)
Les fleurs qu'il a apportéES (fem plural → +es)

WHY? → Same rule as Project 19 (COD before avoir → agreement)
→ QUE acts like a COD pronoun that comes BEFORE avoir

🧠 THE DEFINITIVE TEST:

After the relative pronoun, look at what comes NEXT:

QUI + VERB (directly)       → The antecedent IS the subject
   "L'homme qui PARLE..."

QUE + SUBJECT + VERB        → The antecedent IS the object
   "L'homme que TU VOIS..."

If a VERB follows immediately → QUI
If a NEW SUBJECT follows    → QUE

→ This is the single most reliable shortcut for choosing between qui and que
→ Works in 100% of cases

🧠 CRITICAL RULE — DONT + POSSESSION:

When DONT expresses "whose":
→ The possessive adjective (son, sa, ses) changes to DEFINITE ARTICLE (le, la, les)

BEFORE: Je connais un homme. SA femme est médecin.
AFTER:  Je connais un homme dont LA femme est médecin.
                                  ↑ sa becomes la

WHY? → "dont" already carries the possessive connection ("of whom")
→ Adding "sa" would be double possession — redundant
→ So possessive → definite article

→ ❌ l'homme dont sa femme (WRONG — double possession)
→ ✅ l'homme dont la femme (CORRECT)
