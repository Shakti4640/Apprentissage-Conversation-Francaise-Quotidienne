🟠 PROJECT 37: "Make Someone Do Something"

Managing a team, raising children, getting services done — you don't do it yourself

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You are a project manager in a marketing agency in Paris
→ You have a team of 8 people — designers, writers, developers
→ Your job is NOT to do everything yourself — it's to make things happen through others
→ Your boss asks: "Did you send the report?" → You say: "No — I had Sophie send it."
→ A client asks: "Who designed this?" → You say: "I had it designed by our team."
→ At home, your child won't eat → You make him eat his vegetables
→ You go to a mechanic → You have your car repaired
→ You go to a hairdresser → You get your hair cut
→ In English, these use "make," "have," "get," "let" — all different words
→ In French, ONE structure handles almost all of them: FAIRE + INFINITIF
→ You try: "J'ai fait Sophie envoyer le rapport" — your boss winces
→ The correct version: "J'ai fait envoyer le rapport par Sophie" or "J'ai fait envoyer le rapport à Sophie"
→ The word order, the pronoun placement, and the logic are completely different from English

The core realization:
→ In English: "I made him wait" → Subject + made + PERSON + verb
→ In French: "Je l'ai fait attendre" → Subject + PERSON(pronoun) + faire + infinitif
→ The person who performs the action is NOT the subject — they're the caused agent
→ Faire and the infinitive act as a single unit — nothing separates them
→ This structure is everywhere: parenting, management, services, daily life

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of "faire + infinitif" as a remote control
→ YOU press the button (faire) → SOMEONE ELSE performs the action (infinitif)
→ You are the causer — they are the doer

The Three Core Meanings

→ Faire covers meanings 1 and 2
→ Laisser covers meaning 3 — structurally identical but different intent
→ We'll focus on faire but include laisser in Block I

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

The Basic Formula

SUBJECT + FAIRE (conjugated) + INFINITIF + COMPLEMENT

Key rule: Faire and the infinitive are INSEPARABLE — nothing goes between them

→ ✅ Je fais manger les enfants (I make the children eat)
→ ❌ Je fais les enfants manger (WRONG — nothing between faire and infinitif)

Where Does the "Caused Person" Go?

Case 1 — Only ONE object (the person who does the action)

Je fais travailler Marie.
        │          │
     infinitif   person (direct object)

→ Marie is the COD of faire — she receives the action of "making"
→ Pronoun: Je la fais travailler (P19 — COD pronoun)

Case 2 — TWO objects (person + thing)

Je fais envoyer le rapport à Marie.
           │          │         │
       infinitif    thing    person (indirect — à)

→ "Le rapport" = COD (the thing being sent)
→ "À Marie" = COI (the person being made to send)
→ When BOTH a thing AND a person exist → person becomes COI with à
→ Pronoun: Je le lui fais envoyer (P36 — double pronouns!)

Case 3 — Passive meaning with "par"

Je fais réparer la voiture par le mécanicien.
           │          │              │
       infinitif    thing      agent (by whom)

→ Emphasis on the thing being done — the agent uses par
→ Common for services: having things done BY professionals
→ Pronoun for thing: Je la fais réparer (par le mécanicien)

Pronoun Placement Rule

→ Pronouns go BEFORE faire — not before the infinitive
→ This is different from other modal+infinitif structures (P36: je vais te le dire — pronouns before infinitif)

MODAL + INFINITIF:     Je vais LE faire.      (pronoun before infinitif)
FAIRE + INFINITIF:     Je LE fais réparer.     (pronoun before faire)

→ Why? Because faire+infinitif acts as a SINGLE verbal unit — the pronoun goes before the whole unit

⚠️ Sentence 100 — FAIRE FAIRE: The Double Faire

C'est en faisant faire les choses aux autres qu'on avance.
              │      │
         faire(gérondif)  faire(infinitif)
         "by making"      "do/make"

→ "Faire faire" = to have something made / to make someone do
→ "Je fais faire une robe" = I'm having a dress made
→ "Il a fait faire des travaux" = He had renovations done
→ This is NOT a typo — it's faire (conjugated) + faire (infinitif) = to have someone DO/MAKE something
→ Perfectly grammatical and extremely common

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Memorize the inseparability rule FIRST: faire + infinitif = ONE UNIT — nothing goes between them
→ Master the one-object vs. two-object distinction:

  • One object → person = COD → Je fais travailler Marie → Je la fais travailler
  • Two objects → person = COI (à) → Je fais lire le rapport à Marie → Je le lui fais lire

→ Practice the service meaning — you'll use it daily:

  • Je fais réparer... / Je fais couper... / Je fais livrer... / Je fais installer...

→ Learn se faire early — it covers both chosen services AND suffered events:

  • Se faire couper les cheveux (chosen) / Se faire voler (suffered)

→ Remember fait is INVARIABLE with infinitif — no agreement ever:

  • ✅ Elle s'est fait couper les cheveux (not faite)
  • ✅ Je les ai fait envoyer (not faits)

→ Connect to P36 — double pronoun order applies perfectly inside faire causatif
→ Connect to P19/P20 — single pronoun placement applies but goes before FAIRE, not before infinitif

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ NEVER separate faire from the infinitive:

  • ❌ Je fais Marie travailler → ✅ Je fais travailler Marie
  • ❌ Je fais le rapport envoyer → ✅ Je fais envoyer le rapport
  • English puts the person between "make" and the verb — French NEVER does

→ ❌ NEVER put the pronoun before the infinitive (as with other modals):

  • ❌ Je fais le envoyer → ✅ Je le fais envoyer
  • Compare: Je vais le faire (pronoun before infinitif) vs. Je le fais envoyer (pronoun before faire)

→ ❌ NEVER agree the past participle "fait" when followed by infinitif:

  • ❌ Elle s'est faite couper les cheveux → ✅ Elle s'est fait couper
  • ❌ Les robes que j'ai faites faire → ✅ Les robes que j'ai fait faire
  • This is the ONE exception to normal agreement rules

→ ❌ NEVER confuse faire (force/cause) with laisser (allow):

  • "Je fais manger les enfants" = I MAKE the children eat (they might not want to)
  • "Je laisse manger les enfants" = I LET the children eat (they want to, I allow it)
  • Completely different power dynamics — wrong choice changes meaning dramatically

→ ❌ NEVER use "par" and "à" interchangeably without understanding the difference:

  • "Je fais envoyer le rapport à Marie" = I make Marie send the report (Marie = doer)
  • "Je fais envoyer le rapport par Marie" = I have the report sent by Marie (report = focus)
  • ⚠️ Sometimes ambiguous: "Je fais lire la lettre à Marie" = I make Marie read the letter OR I have the letter read TO Marie → context determines meaning

→ ❌ NEVER use faire causatif when YOU do the action yourself:

  • "I repaired my car" (myself) = J'ai réparé ma voiture
  • "I had my car repaired" (by someone) = J'ai fait réparer ma voiture
  • Adding "faire" means you DIDN'T do it — someone else did

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ When you performed the action yourself → just use the simple verb

  • "I cooked dinner" = J'ai préparé le dîner (not J'ai fait préparer, unless someone else cooked)

→ When expressing passive voice without a causer → use être + participe passé (P24)

  • "The bridge was built in 1900" = Le pont a été construit en 1900
  • NOT faire causatif — there's no specific "causer" being highlighted

→ When the subject causes something TO THEMSELVES reflexively (emotion) → use se faire only for actions, not emotions

  • "I made myself sad" → Je me suis rendu triste (NOT Je me suis fait trister)
  • Se faire works for physical actions and events — not for emotional self-causation

→ When "faire" means "to do/make" literally — not causatively:

  • "Je fais un gâteau" = I'm making a cake (myself — literal faire)
  • "Je fais faire un gâteau" = I'm having a cake made (by someone else — causative faire)
  • One "faire" = doing it yourself / Two "faire" = having it done

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

P36 gave you the ability to stack pronouns — compressing objects into fluid sentences.
P37 gives you the ability to express CAUSATION — making, having, and letting others perform actions.

Faire + infinitif is one of the most distinctly French structures:

  • It has NO direct English equivalent that works the same way
  • Faire and the infinitive are INSEPARABLE — this is non-negotiable
  • Pronouns go before FAIRE — not before the infinitive
  • Past participle "fait" NEVER agrees when followed by an infinitive

The three levels of control:

  • FAIRE = I cause/force it to happen → authority, delegation, services
  • LAISSER = I allow it to happen → permission, freedom, non-interference
  • Simple verb = I do it myself → personal action

You now control WHO does WHAT and WHETHER you're the doer or the causer.
But there's one more way to connect actions: what about doing TWO things AT THE SAME TIME?

🌉 BRIDGE TO PROJECT 38

You can now say "I make someone do something" — one person causes, another acts
But what about expressing simultaneity — two actions happening at the same moment?

→ "I listen to music while working" — two actions, one person, same time
→ "By studying every day, you will improve" — method and result linked
→ "He left smiling" — action + manner happening together

In French: EN + PARTICIPE PRÉSENT = the gérondif
→ "J'écoute de la musique en travaillant"
→ "En étudiant chaque jour, tu progresseras"
→ "Il est parti en souriant"

Project 38 unlocks: Gérondif — expressing simultaneous actions, method, manner, and condition
→ The "-ing" equivalent that French builds completely differently from English
→ Combined with everything learned so far — creating multi-layered, sophisticated sentences
