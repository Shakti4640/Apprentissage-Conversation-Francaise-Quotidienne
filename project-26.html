🟡 PROJECT 26: "You Must, You Have To"

You're explaining rules, responsibilities, duties — at work, at home, in society

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're working in a French company now — your first real job in France
→ Every day, you encounter obligations, rules, duties, prohibitions:

Your boss says:

"Vous devez envoyer le rapport avant vendredi."
"Il faut que tout le monde soit présent à la réunion."

The building sign says:

"Il est interdit de fumer dans les locaux."
"Le port du badge est obligatoire."

Your landlord says:

"Vous êtes tenu de payer le loyer avant le 5 du mois."
"Il ne faut pas faire de bruit après 22h."

Your doctor says:

"Vous devriez arrêter de fumer."
"Il faudrait que vous fassiez plus de sport."

→ You notice something: all these sentences express obligation — but at VERY different levels
→ Some are absolute rules (you MUST)
→ Some are strong duties (you HAVE TO)
→ Some are recommendations (you SHOULD)
→ Some are prohibitions (you MUST NOT)
→ Some are soft suggestions (it would be good to)

→ In English, you switch between "must / have to / should / need to / ought to / are required to"
→ In French, the obligation system is even more layered — and each structure carries a precise weight

→ You try to express obligation — but you only know "il faut" and "devoir":

❌ You use "il faut" for everything — from "you must breathe" to "you should maybe try"
❌ You use "tu dois" for soft advice — and sound like a dictator

The core realization:
→ French has at least 10 distinct obligation structures
→ Each one occupies a different position on the obligation strength scale
→ Choosing the wrong one changes your message — from helpful advice to aggressive command
→ This project maps the entire obligation spectrum from softest suggestion to absolute law

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of obligation in French as a 10-level scale:

LEVEL 1  💨  Soft suggestion          "Tu pourrais..."
LEVEL 2  💨  Gentle advice            "Tu devrais..."
LEVEL 3  🌤️  Recommendation           "Il faudrait..."
LEVEL 4  🌤️  Moral/social expectation "Il est important de..."
LEVEL 5  ☀️  Firm necessity           "Il faut..."
LEVEL 6  ☀️  Personal duty            "Tu dois..."
LEVEL 7  🔥  Strong obligation        "Tu es obligé de..."
LEVEL 8  🔥  Formal requirement       "Vous êtes tenu de..."
LEVEL 9  ⚡  Prohibition              "Il est interdit de..."
LEVEL 10 ⚡  Absolute law             "Il est formellement interdit de..."

→ Your job: Learn every structure at every level — and know when to use which

🧠 THE IL FAUT SYSTEM — Complete:

GENERAL (no specific person):
→ Il faut + INFINITIF
→ "Il faut manger" = One must eat / You have to eat

SPECIFIC PERSON:
→ Il faut que + SUBJECT + SUBJONCTIF
→ "Il faut que JE parte" = I have to leave
→ "Il faut que TU fasses" = You have to do

NEGATIVE (prohibition):
→ Il ne faut pas + INFINITIF
→ "Il ne faut pas mentir" = You must not lie

→ Il ne faut pas que + SUBJECT + SUBJONCTIF
→ "Il ne faut pas que tu sois en retard" = You must not be late

SOFTENED (conditionnel):
→ Il faudrait + INFINITIF (Block B — recommendation level)
→ Il faudrait que + SUBJECT + SUBJONCTIF

PAST:
→ Il fallait + INFINITIF = It was necessary to / You had to
→ Il a fallu + INFINITIF = It was necessary to (completed event)
→ Il fallait que + SUBJONCTIF = You had to (specific person, past)

🧠 CRITICAL WARNING — "Il ne faut pas" vs. "Il n'est pas nécessaire de":

IL NE FAUT PAS = You MUST NOT (PROHIBITION — strong)
→ "Il ne faut pas mentir" = You must not lie (forbidden)

IL N'EST PAS NÉCESSAIRE DE = It's not necessary to (NO OBLIGATION — but allowed)
→ "Il n'est pas nécessaire de réserver" = You don't need to book (but you can)

English "don't have to" = il n'est pas nécessaire de / ce n'est pas obligatoire
English "must not" = il ne faut pas

THIS IS A MAJOR TRAP — many learners confuse them:
❌ "Il ne faut pas réserver" ≠ "You don't have to book"
✅ "Il ne faut pas réserver" = "You must NOT book" (prohibition!)
✅ "Ce n'est pas obligatoire de réserver" = "You don't have to book"

🧠 CRITICAL DISTINCTION — "Devais" vs. "Ai dû":

→ "Je devais travailler, mais j'ai décidé de rester."

J'AI DÛ = I had to (COMPLETED — I actually did it)
→ "J'ai dû annuler mon rendez-vous."

🧠 KEY DISTINCTIONS — The Formal Trio:

ÊTRE OBLIGÉ(E) DE = to be obliged/required to
→ External constraint — someone or something FORCES you
→ "Je suis obligé de travailler le samedi" = I'm forced to work Saturdays
→ Can be physical, legal, or social pressure
→ NEGATIVE: "Tu n'es pas obligé de..." = You don't HAVE to... (no obligation)

ÊTRE TENU(E) DE = to be required/bound to
→ LEGAL / OFFICIAL obligation — contracts, laws, rules
→ "Vous êtes tenu de respecter..." = You are bound to respect...
→ Much more formal than "être obligé de"
→ Found in legal documents, contracts, official regulations

ÊTRE CENSÉ(E) + infinitif = to be supposed to / expected to
→ Social expectation — not necessarily a hard rule
→ "Tu es censé arriver à 9h" = You're SUPPOSED to arrive at 9
→ Implies: maybe you don't always do it — but you SHOULD
→ Very common in daily conversation
→ NOTE: No "de" — directly followed by infinitif

→ ❌ Do NOT mix up "devoir" meanings

DEVOIR has THREE possible meanings depending on context:

1. OBLIGATION:  Je dois partir. = I must leave.
2. PROBABILITY: Il doit être malade. = He must be sick (probably is).
3. DEBT:        Je te dois 10 euros. = I owe you 10 euros.

Context makes it clear — but be aware of the ambiguity
