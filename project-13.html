🔵 PROJECT 13: "Tell Them What to Do"

You're cooking with a friend, giving directions, offering advice — how do you give commands?

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ It's Saturday evening at your host family's kitchen in Lyon
→ The mother, Catherine, is teaching you to make crêpes — a classic French recipe
→ She speaks rapidly, firing off instructions:
→ → "Mélange la farine et les œufs !" (Mix the flour and eggs!)
→ → "Ajoute du lait !" (Add some milk!)
→ → "Ne mets pas trop de sucre !" (Don't put too much sugar!)
→ → "Attends cinq minutes !" (Wait five minutes!)
→ → "Goûte !" (Taste!)
→ You understand WHAT she wants — but you notice something strange:
→ → There's no "tu" or "vous" in these sentences
→ → The verbs look ALMOST like present tense — but not exactly
→ → Some have an "s" missing that you'd expect
→ Later, you're walking with friends and need to say:
→ → "Let's go!" / "Turn left!" / "Don't touch that!" / "Listen to me!"
→ You can describe plans (Project 12), tell stories (Project 11), ask questions (Project 4)
→ But you CANNOT directly instruct, command, suggest, or guide someone

The core realization:
→ The impératif (imperative) is the command form of verbs
→ It's used for: orders, instructions, advice, suggestions, invitations, recipes, directions
→ It has only 3 forms — the simplest conjugation in all of French:

FormWho You're Talking ToExample
Tu formOne person (informal)Mange ! (Eat!)
Nous form"Let's..." (including yourself)Mangeons ! (Let's eat!)
Vous formOne person (formal) OR a groupMangez ! (Eat!)

→ The KEY difference from present tense: NO subject pronoun
→ Present: Tu manges. (You eat.)
→ Impératif: Mange ! (Eat!) — no "tu", no "vous"
→ The missing subject IS the command — you're speaking directly to someone

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ The impératif is built directly from present tense forms (Project 2)
→ With ONE important twist for -ER verbs

Verb TypePresent "tu" FormImpératif "tu" FormWhat Changed?
-ER verbsTu parlesParle !Drop the -s
-IR verbsTu finisFinis !Keep the -s
-RE verbsTu attendsAttends !Keep the -s

→ The nous and vous forms are IDENTICAL to present tense — no changes at all
→ The only special rule: -ER verbs (and aller) lose the -s in the tu form

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

Step 1 — Regular -ER Verbs in Impératif

Present tense tu form → DROP the -s → Impératif

VerbPresent (tu)Impératif (tu)Impératif (nous)Impératif (vous)
ParlerTu parlesParle !Parlons !Parlez !
MangerTu mangesMange !Mangeons !Mangez !
ÉcouterTu écoutesÉcoute !Écoutons !Écoutez !
RegarderTu regardesRegarde !Regardons !Regardez !
GoûterTu goûtesGoûte !Goûtons !Goûtez !
AjouterTu ajoutesAjoute !Ajoutons !Ajoutez !

→ The -s disappears in the tu form for ALL -ER verbs
→ This is the ONLY change — nous and vous forms are identical to present tense
→ Why drop the -s? Historical reasons — but the practical effect: it sounds exactly the same in speech
→ → "Tu parles" and "Parle !" sound identical: "parl"
→ → The difference is only visible in writing

Step 2 — Regular -IR and -RE Verbs in Impératif

Present tense forms → NO changes → Impératif

VerbImpératif (tu)Impératif (nous)Impératif (vous)
FinirFinis !Finissons !Finissez !
ChoisirChoisis !Choisissons !Choisissez !
AttendreAttends !Attendons !Attendez !
DormirDors !Dormons !Dormez !
PartirPars !Partons !Partez !
SortirSors !Sortons !Sortez !
PrendrePrends !Prenons !Prenez !

→ -IR and -RE verbs: keep everything from present tense — just remove the subject pronoun
→ The -s stays for tu forms of -IR and -RE verbs

Step 3 — Irregular Impératif: The Big 4

Four verbs have SPECIAL impératif forms that DON'T come from present tense:

ÊTRE

FormImpératif
TuSois !
NousSoyons !
VousSoyez !

→ NOT from present tense (tu es → Es !)
→ Comes from the subjonctif stem (you'll learn this in Project 23/31)
→ For now: just memorize sois, soyons, soyez

AVOIR

FormImpératif
TuAie !
NousAyons !
VousAyez !

→ NOT from present tense (tu as → As !)
→ Also from subjonctif stem
→ Less common in daily speech — but appears in fixed expressions

SAVOIR

FormImpératif
TuSache !
NousSachons !
VousSachez !

→ NOT from present tense (tu sais → Sais !)
→ Used in formal contexts: "Sachez que..." (Know that... / Be aware that...)

ALLER (special tu form only)

FormImpératif
TuVa !
NousAllons !
VousAllez !

→ Tu form: Va ! (not Vas !) — the -s is dropped (same rule as -ER verbs — aller ends in -er)
→ Nous and vous: regular present tense forms → allons, allez
→ "Allez !" and "Allons !" are among the most common words in spoken French
→ → "Allez !" = "Come on!" / "Go!" / "Go ahead!"
→ → "Allons-y !" = "Let's go!"

Step 4 — The -S Returns Before Y and EN

Special pronunciation rule:

→ When the tu impératif of an -ER verb is followed by "y" or "en", the -s comes back

Without Y/ENWith YWith EN
Mange ! (Eat!)Manges-en ! (Eat some!)—
Va ! (Go!)Vas-y ! (Go ahead! / Go there!)—
Parle ! (Speak!)—Parles-en ! (Talk about it!)

→ Why? Pronunciation — French avoids the collision of two vowel sounds
→ "Va" + "y" → "Va-y" sounds awkward → "Vas-y" flows better
→ The -s acts as a liaison sound between the verb and y/en
→ "Vas-y !" = one of the TOP 5 most used expressions in spoken French
→ → Means: "Go ahead!" / "Go for it!" / "Do it!" / "Come on!"

Step 5 — Negative Impératif

NE + VERB (impératif) + PAS + rest

→ Same negation sandwich (Project 5) — wraps around the imperative verb
→ "Ne" before → "Pas" after

AffirmativeNegative
Mange ! (Eat!)Ne mange pas ! (Don't eat!)
Pars ! (Leave!)Ne pars pas ! (Don't leave!)
Touche ! (Touch!)Ne touche pas ! (Don't touch!)
Sois triste ! (Be sad!)Ne sois pas triste ! (Don't be sad!)

→ Spoken French: "ne" drops as usual
→ Written: Ne mange pas ! → Spoken: Mange pas !

🧠 Sentence 18 — OUVRIR in impératif:
→ Ouvrir conjugates like an -ER verb in present: tu ouvres
→ So impératif tu form: Ouvre ! (drop the -s, like -ER verbs)
→ Same for: couvrir (cover) → Couvre ! / offrir (offer) → Offre ! / découvrir (discover) → Découvre !
→ These -IR verbs that conjugate like -ER verbs follow -ER impératif rules

🧠 Sentence 19 — "DIS-MOI":
→ Dis (tell) + moi (me) = Tell me
→ In affirmative impératif, pronouns go AFTER the verb with a hyphen
→ This is a MAJOR difference from normal sentence structure
→ Normal: Tu me dis. (You tell me.) → pronoun BEFORE verb
→ Impératif: Dis-moi ! (Tell me!) → pronoun AFTER verb
→ Full pronoun exploration in Project 19 — for now, absorb these common patterns:

🧠 Sentence 40 — "ALLONS-Y !":
→ One of the most famous French expressions
→ Allons (let's go) + y (there) = Let's go (there)!
→ Used to signal "Let's do this!" / "Let's get started!" / "Here we go!"
→ Compare with "Vas-y !" (Go ahead! — tu form from Step 4)

🧠 "On" vs. "Nous" for suggestions:
→ In daily speech, the "nous" impératif is sometimes replaced by "On + present" as a suggestion:
→ → "Allons au cinéma !" (impératif — slightly formal)
→ → "On va au cinéma ?" (present with question — very casual suggestion)
→ → "On y va ?" (Let's go? — very casual)
→ The impératif nous form is correct and natural but more decisive
→ The "on" question form is softer — more like a suggestion than a command

→ Remember -IR and -RE keep the -s in tu form → Tu finis → Finis ! / Tu attends → Attends !
→ Memorize the 4 irregulars: être (sois/soyons/soyez), avoir (aie/ayons/ayez), savoir (sache/sachons/sachez), aller (va/allons/allez)
→ Learn "Vas-y !" and "Allons-y !" — you'll use them daily
→ Add "s'il te plaît / s'il vous plaît" to soften any command into a polite request
→ Learn the discourse markers (Tiens ! Voyons... Écoute... Allez ! Dis donc !) — they're everywhere in spoken French
→ Practice negative impératif — Ne + verb + pas — same sandwich as always
→ Remember pronoun position flip — affirmative: after verb (Dis-moi) / negative: before verb (Ne me dis pas)
→ Connect to Project 2 — impératif forms come directly from present tense
→ Connect to Project 5 — negation rules are identical
→ Connect to Project 7 — direction vocabulary (à gauche, à droite, tout droit) combines with impératif

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT keep the -s in tu form of -ER verbs → "Manges !" → "Mange !"
→ ❌ EXCEPT before y and en → "Va-y !" → "Vas-y !" (the -s returns)
→ ❌ Do NOT use subject pronouns → "Tu mange !" → "Mange !" (no tu)
→ ❌ Do NOT use impératif with il/elle/ils/elles → there's no third-person imperative in French
→ → You can't say "Mange il !" → use "Qu'il mange !" (subjunctive — Project 31)
→ ❌ Do NOT use present tense forms for être/avoir/savoir impératif → "Es courageux !" → "Sois courageux !"
→ ❌ Do NOT put pronouns BEFORE the verb in affirmative impératif → "Me dis !" → "Dis-moi !"
→ ❌ Do NOT put pronouns AFTER the verb in negative impératif → "Ne dis-moi pas !" → "Ne me dis pas !"
→ ❌ Do NOT use impératif for polite requests without softening → bare commands to strangers can sound rude → add s'il vous plaît
→ ❌ Do NOT confuse impératif with infinitif in written instructions → Recipe books use BOTH:
→ → Impératif: "Mélangez la farine." (addressed to "you")

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Do NOT use impératif when you should be polite and indirect → with strangers, use conditionnel (Project 17): "Pourriez-vous fermer la porte ?" instead of "Fermez la porte !"
→ Do NOT use impératif for third persons (he, she, they) → French has no third-person imperative → use subjunctive construction (Project 31): "Qu'il parte !" (Let him leave!)
→ Do NOT use impératif for past commands → "I told you to eat" = reported speech (Project 25), not impératif
→ Do NOT use impératif when making general rules → use "il faut" (Project 6): "Il faut manger" (One must eat) vs. "Mange !" (Eat! — directed at someone)
→ Do NOT use impératif to describe what you do → "Je mange" (I eat) ≠ "Mange !" (Eat!)
→ Do NOT overuse impératif in French culture → French people soften commands more than English speakers → use questions, conditionnel, or s'il vous plaît frequently

Project 13 → TELLING people what to do (impératif)

The impératif has only 3 forms — the simplest conjugation in French:
→ Tu form: Present tu minus subject (and minus -s for -ER verbs)
→ Nous form: Present nous minus subject → "Let's..."
→ Vous form: Present vous minus subject → polite/plural commands

Four irregular verbs: être (sois), avoir (aie), savoir (sache), aller (va)

Key rules:

  1. No subject pronoun in impératif
  2. -ER verbs drop the -s in tu form (but it returns before y/en)
  3. Pronouns flip position: after verb (affirmative) / before verb (negative)
  4. Add s'il te/vous plaît to soften any command into a polite request

You can now DIRECT people:
→ Give instructions, directions, advice, encouragement, warnings
→ Suggest activities ("Let's go!"), refuse politely ("Don't worry!")

But you keep saying "je" for everything:
→ "I wash" / "I get up" / "I dress" — English uses separate words
→ French uses reflexive verbs — "je ME lave" / "je ME lève" / "je M'habille"
→ These verbs where the action reflects back onto yourself are EVERYWHERE in daily French
