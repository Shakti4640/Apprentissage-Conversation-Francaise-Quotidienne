🟠 PROJECT 36: "Stack the Pronouns"

A busy office day where everything has already been mentioned — and you must stop repeating yourself

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You work in a busy office in Lyon — phones ringing, emails flying, colleagues asking questions
→ Your boss asks: "Tu as envoyé le rapport à Marie ?"
→ You want to say: "Yes, I sent it to her" — but in French, that means replacing BOTH "le rapport" (COD) AND "à Marie" (COI) with pronouns
→ You try: "Oui, je l'ai envoyé à elle" — your boss frowns — it sounds clunky and unnatural
→ The correct answer: "Oui, je le lui ai envoyé."
→ Two pronouns. Before the verb. In a specific order. No hesitation.
→ A colleague asks: "Tu as parlé du problème au directeur ?"
→ You need to replace "du problème" (EN — P20) and "au directeur" (COI — P19) → "Je lui en ai parlé"
→ Another asks: "Tu m'as apporté les dossiers ?" → "Oui, je te les ai apportés."
→ You realize: every efficient French sentence in professional life stacks pronouns
→ Natives do it instinctively — you must learn the precise stacking order

The core realization:
→ P19 taught you to replace ONE object with a pronoun (COD or COI)
→ P20 taught you Y and EN as standalone replacements
→ P36 teaches you to combine TWO pronouns in the same sentence — and the ORDER is non-negotiable

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of French double pronouns like a seating chart — each pronoun has an assigned seat before the verb
→ If you put them in the wrong order, the sentence is immediately ungrammatical
→ Natives will understand you, but it will sound like putting the adjective before the noun in English — jarring

The Master Pronoun Order Chart

POSITION 1  →  POSITION 2  →  POSITION 3  →  POSITION 4  →  POSITION 5  →  VERB
   me (m')        te (t')         le (l')        lui            y              
   nous            vous            la (l')        leur           en             
   se (s')                         les

Simplified rule — memorize these COLUMNS:

Column 1Column 2Column 3Column 4
me, te, se, nous, vousle, la, leslui, leury, en

→ Column 1 comes before Column 2
→ Column 2 comes before Column 3
→ Column 3 comes before Column 4
→ Y always comes before EN (when both appear — rare but possible)

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

Step 1 — Identify WHAT needs replacing

→ Find the COD (direct object — answers "what?" or "whom?")
→ Find the COI (indirect object — answers "to whom?" or "for whom?")
→ Find if EN (replaces "de + noun" / quantity) or Y (replaces "à + place" / "à + thing") applies
→ Replace each with its pronoun
→ Stack them in the correct column order

Step 2 — Place them correctly based on tense

Tense TypePronoun PlacementExample
Simple tense (présent, imparfait, futur)Before conjugated verbJe le lui donne
Compound tense (passé composé, pqp, cond.passé)Before auxiliary (avoir/être)Je le lui ai donné
Near future (aller + inf)Before infinitiveJe vais le lui donner
Modal + infinitive (pouvoir, devoir, vouloir + inf)Before infinitiveJe peux le lui donner
Imperative affirmativeAfter verb with hyphens — ORDER CHANGESDonne-le-lui
Imperative negativeBefore verb — normal orderNe le lui donne pas

→ Critical: In affirmative imperative, the order reverses — this is the major exception
→ We'll cover this in Block H

→ In affirmative imperative:

  • "me" becomes "moi" → Donne-le-moi (not Donne-le-me)
  • "te" becomes "toi" → Donne-le-toi (not Donne-le-te)
  • EXCEPT before "en": "me" stays as "m'" → Donne-**m'**en (not Donne-moi-en)

→ In negative imperative: everything goes back to normal pre-verb position — as if the imperative exception doesn't exist

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Memorize the column order FIRST — before any sentences:

me/te/se/nous/vous → le/la/les → lui/leur → y/en

→ Start with the 4 most common combinations — these cover 90% of daily use:

  1. me/te + le/la/les → "Il me le donne"
  2. le/la/les + lui/leur → "Je le lui envoie"
  3. me/te + en → "Il m'en parle"
  4. lui/leur + en → "Je lui en donne"

→ Practice the ONE verb across all tenses technique (Block G) — take "dire" with "te le" through every tense until automatic

→ Master the imperative flip — this is the #1 source of errors:

  • Affirmative: Donne-le-moi (after verb, hyphens, moi not me)
  • Negative: Ne me le donne pas (before verb, normal order, me not moi)

→ Always check agreement in compound tenses — the COD pronoun (le/la/les) triggers it

→ Connect to P19 — single pronoun placement → P36 doubles it
→ Connect to P20 — Y and EN placement → P36 stacks them with other pronouns

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ NEVER reverse column order:

  • ❌ Je le te donne → ✅ Je te le donne (Column 1 before Column 2)
  • ❌ Je lui le donne → ✅ Je le lui donne (Column 2 before Column 3)
  • ❌ Je en lui parle → ✅ Je lui en parle (Column 3 before Column 4)

→ ❌ NEVER use "lui + y" in speech:

  • ❌ Je lui y emmène → ✅ Je l'y emmène / Je l'emmène là-bas

→ ❌ NEVER put double pronouns AFTER the verb in declarative sentences:

  • ❌ Je donne le lui → ✅ Je le lui donne

→ ❌ NEVER forget moi/toi in affirmative imperative:

  • ❌ Donne-le-me → ✅ Donne-le-moi
  • ❌ Donne-le-te → ✅ Donne-le-toi

→ ❌ NEVER use "me" or "te" before "en" in affirmative imperative — use m'/t':

  • ❌ Donne-moi-en → ✅ Donne-m'en
  • ❌ Parle-toi-en → ✅ (this combination rarely occurs — restructure)

→ ❌ NEVER trigger agreement from a COI pronoun:

  • ❌ Je lui ai envoyée la lettre → COI (lui) does NOT cause agreement
  • ✅ Je lui ai envoyé la lettre (no agreement — COD follows verb)
  • BUT: La lettre, je la lui ai envoyée (agreement because COD "la" precedes auxiliary)

→ ❌ NEVER trigger agreement from EN:

  • ❌ Des erreurs, j'en ai faites → EN does not trigger agreement (special rule)
  • ✅ Des erreurs, j'en ai fait

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ When the sentence has only ONE object → use single pronoun placement (P19/P20)

  • "I see him" = Je le vois — no need for double pronouns
  • Only use double when TWO objects are being replaced simultaneously

→ When clarity is at risk → keep one full noun, replace the other

  • "Je le lui ai donné" can be ambiguous — "lui" = him or her?
  • If unclear → "J'ai donné le dossier à Marie" or "Je le lui ai donné, à Marie" (add clarifier)

→ When writing formal/literary French → double pronouns can feel too compressed

  • Formal writing sometimes prefers: "Je l'ai transmis à Monsieur le Directeur" over "Je le lui ai transmis"
  • Spoken French loves compression — written French sometimes prefers clarity

→ When three objects need replacing → NEVER stack three pronouns

  • ❌ Je te le lui y en donne → Impossible — maximum TWO pronouns before the verb
  • Restructure: keep one as a full noun phrase

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

P19 gave you the ability to replace ONE object with a pronoun.
P20 gave you Y and EN as powerful replacements.
P36 teaches you to COMBINE two pronouns — making your French compressed, fluid, and native-sounding.

The column order is absolute:

me/te/se/nous/vous → le/la/les → lui/leur → y → en → VERB

The imperative is the ONE exception where order flips — and where "me" becomes "moi."

Double pronouns are what separate a learner who speaks correct French from a speaker who sounds fluent.
Without them, you repeat nouns constantly.
With them, your sentences become tight, efficient, and natural.

But what happens when YOU don't do the action — you make SOMEONE ELSE do it?
"I cut my hair" vs. "I had my hair cut" — two completely different structures.

🌉 BRIDGE TO PROJECT 37

You now stack pronouns inside any sentence — your objects are compressed
But there's a verb structure where the subject does NOT perform the action — they CAUSE it

→ "I repaired my car" = I did it myself
→ "I had my car repaired" = someone else did it — I caused it to happen

In French: Faire + infinitif — the causative construction
→ "Je fais réparer ma voiture" = I'm having my car repaired
→ "Elle fait manger les enfants" = She feeds the children (makes them eat)
→ "Il m'a fait attendre" = He made me wait

Project 37 unlocks: Faire causatif — making, having, getting someone to do something
→ Combined with pronouns from P36 — "Je la lui fais envoyer" = I'm having her send it to him
→ The structure that controls delegation, authority, and causation in French
