🟢 PROJECT 8: "When Things Happen"

You're scheduling your life — how do you express hours, days, months, seasons, and frequency?

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You've enrolled in a French language school in Paris — classes start next week
→ The school sends you a schedule — you need to understand it and discuss it
→ Your host family asks about your weekly routine — when you have class, when you're free
→ You want to make plans with new friends — "Let's meet on Saturday" / "I'm free in the afternoon"
→ You need to book a doctor's appointment — "Is Tuesday at 3 PM available?"
→ You can now say who you are (P1), what you do (P2), identify things (P3), ask questions (P4), say no (P5), express wants/needs (P6), and locate yourself in space (P7)
→ But EVERY sentence is stuck in a timeless void
→ You cannot say:
→ → "I work on Mondays."
→ → "My class starts at 9 o'clock."
→ → "I arrived in September."
→ → "I always eat breakfast."
→ → "I sometimes go to the cinema."
→ → "It's cold in winter."
→ → "Today is Friday."
→ → "I'm busy this week."

The core realization:
→ English time markers are chaotic — "in March" but "on Monday" but "at 3 PM"
→ French time markers follow clearer patterns — but they're DIFFERENT patterns from English
→ The prepositions for time are NOT the same as prepositions for place (Project 7)
→ "En" for place = in a feminine country → "En" for time = in a month/season
→ "À" for place = in a city → "À" for time = at a specific hour
→ "Le" for time = on a recurring day → English doesn't use "the" for days
→ If you confuse place prepositions with time prepositions → your sentences collapse

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Time in French is organized into 5 layers:

LayerWhat It CoversKey Markers
Layer 1Clock time (hours, minutes)à, heure(s), et quart, et demie, moins
Layer 2Parts of the dayle matin, l'après-midi, le soir, la nuit
Layer 3Days of the weeklundi, mardi... + le for recurring
Layer 4Months, seasons, yearsen, au, months, seasons
Layer 5Frequency & relative timetoujours, souvent, parfois, jamais, aujourd'hui, demain, hier

→ Each layer has its own preposition rules
→ You also need to know how to tell time — French uses a 24-hour clock AND a 12-hour conversational style

→ Schedules/TV/trains → 24-hour (quinze heures, vingt heures)
→ Conversation → 12-hour with context (trois heures de l'après-midi)

Step 2 — "At" a Time: À + heure

EVENT + à + TIME

→ Je commence à neuf heures. (I start at nine o'clock.)
→ Le train part à midi. (The train leaves at noon.)
→ On se retrouve à huit heures et demie. (We meet at 8:30.)

→ "À" for time = "at" — same preposition as "à" for cities (Project 7) but different function
→ Context always makes it clear: à Paris (place) vs. à huit heures (time)

Step 7 — Frequency Words

Placement rule:

→ Frequency words go AFTER the conjugated verb in French

Subject + VERB + FREQUENCY + rest

→ Je mange toujours à midi. (I always eat at noon.)
→ Il sort souvent le soir. (He often goes out in the evening.)
→ On va parfois au cinéma. (We sometimes go to the cinema.)
→ Elle arrive rarement en retard. (She rarely arrives late.)
→ Je ne dors jamais avant minuit. (I never sleep before midnight.)

→ In English, frequency words often go BEFORE the verb → "I always eat"
→ In French, they go AFTER → "Je mange toujours"
→ This word order difference is a common error for English speakers

🧠 The "moins" system:
→ French thinks of time BEFORE the next hour:
→ 8:45 = "nine o'clock minus a quarter" = neuf heures moins le quart
→ 8:50 = "nine o'clock minus ten" = neuf heures moins dix
→ 8:40 = "nine o'clock minus twenty" = neuf heures moins vingt
→ Generally, "moins" is used for the last 15-20 minutes before the next hour
→ After the half hour → start thinking toward the NEXT hour

🧠 CRITICAL: DEPUIS vs. POUR vs. PENDANT

WordMeaningWhen to UseTense Used
DepuisSince / ForAction started in past and still continuing NOWPresent tense in French!
PourForPlanned future durationPresent or future
PendantFor / DuringCompleted duration or general durationAny tense

→ The DEPUIS trap:
→ English: "I have been living here for six months." (present perfect)
→ French: "J'habite ici depuis six mois." (present tense!)
→ French uses PRESENT TENSE with depuis when the action is STILL happening
→ This is a MAJOR difference from English — one of the hardest adjustments

English (present perfect)French (present + depuis)
I have been waiting for 20 minutes.J'attends depuis 20 minutes.
She has been living here since 2020.Elle habite ici depuis 2020.
We have been studying French for a year.On étudie le français depuis un an.

→ Do NOT use past tense with depuis (at this level) → "J'ai habité depuis six mois" ❌
→ Use PRESENT tense → "J'habite depuis six mois" ✅

→ Master "à + time" — you will use it in every scheduling conversation
→ Memorize the LE + day = recurring rule — this tiny article changes meaning completely
→ Learn "en + month" and the season prepositions (au printemps, en for the rest)
→ Internalize frequency word placement — AFTER the verb in French, not before like English
→ Practice "depuis + present tense" — this is one of the hardest habits to build for English speakers
→ Know the difference between depuis / pour / pendant — they all translate to "for" but function differently
→ Connect to Project 7 — preposition logic carries over: "en" for countries AND months, "à" for cities AND clock times
→ Connect to Project 4 — "À quelle heure ?" / "Quel jour ?" / "C'est quand ?" are your time questions
→ Connect to Project 2 — your daily routine verbs now get time anchors: "Je mange (P2) à midi (P8)"

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT say "dans mars" or "à mars" → "en mars"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "en printemps" → "au printemps" (only season with au)
→ ❌ Do NOT say "à le lundi" → just "le lundi" (no preposition needed for days!)
→ ❌ Do NOT put frequency words before the verb → "Je toujours mange" → "Je mange toujours"
→ ❌ Do NOT use past tense with "depuis" for ongoing actions → "J'ai habité depuis six mois" → "J'habite depuis six mois"
→ ❌ Do NOT capitalize days or months → "Lundi" → "lundi" / "Mars" → "mars"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "le deuxième mars" for dates → "le deux mars" (cardinal numbers for dates)
→ ❌ Do NOT say "douze heures" for noon in conversation → "midi"
→ ❌ Do NOT confuse "ce matin" (this morning) with "le matin" (mornings in general / every morning)
→ ❌ Do NOT say "pour six mois" when the action is still happening → use "depuis six mois"

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Do NOT use à for months → à = clock time and cities only → use en for months
→ Do NOT use en for clock time → "en huit heures" means "in eight hours" (duration!) not "at eight o'clock"
→ Do NOT use dans for months or seasons → dans = physical containment or future duration ("dans deux jours" = in two days — different!)
→ Do NOT use le before "aujourd'hui, demain, hier" → these are standalone time words → "le aujourd'hui" ❌
→ Do NOT use depuis for actions that have STOPPED → use pendant → "J'ai travaillé pendant deux ans" (I worked for two years — finished)
→ Do NOT use pour for actions still in progress → use depuis → "Je suis ici depuis trois jours" (I've been here for three days — still here)
