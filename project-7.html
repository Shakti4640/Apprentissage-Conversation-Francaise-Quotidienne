🟢 PROJECT 7: "Here, There, To, From"

You must explain where you live, where you're going, where you come from

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're at a welcome dinner organized by a French language school in Paris
→ Students from all over the world are introducing themselves
→ Everyone is saying where they come from, where they live now, and where they want to go
→ You hear:
→ → "Je viens du Brésil." / "J'habite en France." / "Je vais à Lyon."
→ → "Elle habite au Japon." / "Il vient des États-Unis." / "On va chez Pierre."
→ You're confused — why is it sometimes à, sometimes en, sometimes au, sometimes aux?
→ Why du sometimes and de other times?
→ What is chez — you've never seen it in English
→ You try to say "I live in France" and you say "J'habite à France" — WRONG
→ You try to say "I'm going to the office" and say "Je vais à le bureau" — WRONG
→ The preposition system for places is one of the most error-prone areas for learners
→ It has clear rules — but the rules depend on the type of place (city, country, continent, building, person's home)

The core realization:
→ English uses "in" and "to" for almost everything → "I live in France" / "I'm going to France"
→ French uses DIFFERENT prepositions depending on:
→ → Is it a city or a country?
→ → Is the country masculine or feminine?
→ → Does the country start with a vowel?
→ → Is it plural (like "les États-Unis")?
→ → Are you going TO a place or coming FROM a place?
→ → Is it a building or a person's home?

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ French prepositions of place follow a decision tree:

WHAT KIND OF PLACE?
├── CITY → à (to/in) / de (from)
├── COUNTRY
│   ├── Feminine or vowel-start → en (to/in) / de/d' (from)
│   ├── Masculine consonant-start → au (to/in) / du (from)
│   └── Plural → aux (to/in) / des (from)
├── BUILDING / SPECIFIC PLACE → à + article / de + article
└── PERSON'S HOME → chez

→ You also need location words (here, there, near, far, next to, in front of, behind)
→ These attach to the end of sentences and tell you WHERE exactly

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

Step 1 — Prepositions with CITIES

Rule: Cities are SIMPLE

DirectionPrepositionExample
To / In a cityàJe vais à Paris. / J'habite à Lyon.
From a cityde (or d' before vowel)Je viens de Paris. / Je viens **d'**Amsterdam.

→ No gender, no article — just à and de
→ Exception: very few cities have built-in articles: Le Havre, Le Caire, La Nouvelle-Orléans
→ → Je vais au Havre. (à + Le = au)
→ → Je viens du Caire. (de + Le = du)
→ At this level: focus on standard cities — exceptions are rare

Step 2 — Prepositions with COUNTRIES

Rule: Countries have GENDER — and gender determines the preposition

How to know a country's gender:

PatternGenderExamples
Ends in -eUsually feminineLa France, La Chine, L'Italie, La Russie
Ends in anything elseUsually masculineLe Japon, Le Brésil, Le Canada, Le Maroc
ExceptionMasculine despite -eLe Mexique, Le Mozambique, Le Cambodge
Plural countriesPluralLes États-Unis, Les Pays-Bas, Les Philippines

The preposition rules:

Country TypeTO / INFROMExample TOExample FROM

PluralauxdesJe vais aux États-Unis.Je viens des États-Unis.

→ Continents follow the SAME rules as feminine countries (all continents are feminine):
→ → en Europe, en Asie, en Afrique, en Amérique

Step 3 — Prepositions with BUILDINGS / SPECIFIC PLACES

Rule: Use À + ARTICLE

→ Recall from Project 3: articles are le, la, l', les
→ When à meets le or les → they CONTRACT:

→ Same logic for FROM with de:

CombinationContractionExample

→ These contractions are mandatory — you CANNOT say "à le" or "de le"
→ You already saw this in Project 3 (articles) and Project 2 (aller au cinéma) — now it's formalized

→ English has NO equivalent preposition

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Memorize the country gender rule: ends in -e = feminine (en/de) → everything else = masculine (au/du) → plural = aux/des
→ Memorize the contractions — à + le = au / de + le = du / à + les = aux / de + les = des — these are MANDATORY
→ Learn "chez" with stress pronouns — chez moi, chez toi, chez lui... — you will use these daily
→ Distinguish dans/en/à for "in" — physical container (dans), country (en), city or building (à)
→ Practice location words with de — près de, loin de, à côté de, en face de — and apply contraction rules
→ Connect to Project 2 — aller (je vais au bureau), habiter (j'habite à Paris), venir (je viens du Brésil)
→ Connect to Project 3 — articles (le, la, l', les) are the same ones that contract with à and de
→ Connect to Project 6 — modals + prepositions: "Je veux aller en France" / "Je dois aller chez le médecin"

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT say "à le" → always contract to "au"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "de le" → always contract to "du"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "J'habite à France" → France is feminine → "J'habite en France"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "J'habite en Paris" → Paris is a city → "J'habite à Paris"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "Je vais à chez Pierre" → chez is already a preposition → "Je vais chez Pierre" (no à)
→ ❌ Do NOT say "en le Japon" → masculine country → "au Japon"
→ ❌ Do NOT use "dans" for countries → "dans la France" → "en France"
→ ❌ Do NOT use regular subject pronouns after chez → "chez je" → "chez moi"
→ ❌ Do NOT forget de in location expressions → "à côté la banque" → "à côté de la banque"

The preposition system is a decision tree:
→ City? → à / de
→ Country? → Check gender → en/de (fem) or au/du (masc) or aux/des (plural)
