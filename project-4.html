🟢 PROJECT 4: "Asking Without Sounding Lost"

You need information — directions, prices, times — how do you ask properly?

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're walking through le Vieux Lyon (Old Lyon) — the historic district
→ You're lost — you need to find a pharmacy, a metro station, a restaurant
→ You want to know prices, times, directions, reasons, names
→ You can identify things (Project 3), describe your routine (Project 2), and say who you are (Project 1)
→ But every time you need information, you freeze
→ You don't know how to form a question in French
→ You default to pointing and saying single words: "Pharmacie ?" "Métro ?" "Combien ?"
→ This works for survival — but you sound like a tourist reading a phrasebook
→ French has THREE different ways to ask the same question — and each one signals a different social register

The core realization:
→ In English, questions are formed mainly by word order change: "You are" → "Are you?"
→ In French, you have three systems — and the one you choose tells the listener whether you're being casual, neutral, or formal
→ Choosing the wrong register doesn't create a grammar error — it creates a social error
→ A tourist asking a shopkeeper using inversion sounds strangely formal
→ A professional using only intonation in a business meeting sounds uneducated

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ French questions are built using two ingredients:

Ingredient 1Ingredient 2
Question FORM (how you structure it)Question WORD (what information you want)

→ There are 3 forms and 8 main question words
→ Any question word can be used with any form
→ This gives you an enormous matrix of possibilities

The 3 Question Forms

FormNameRegisterHow It WorksExample
Form 1IntonationCasual / SpokenSame word order as statement → voice goes UP at endTu parles français ? ↗
Form 2Est-ce queNeutral / StandardAdd "Est-ce que" at the beginning → normal word orderEst-ce que tu parles français ?
Form 3InversionFormal / WrittenFlip subject and verb → add hyphenParles-tu français ?

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

Step 1 — Yes/No Questions (No question word needed)

These are questions where the answer is simply oui or non

Form 1 — Intonation (Casual)

→ Take any statement from Projects 1-3 → raise your voice at the end

Form 2 — Est-ce que (Standard)

→ Put "Est-ce que" before the statement → keep everything else the same

Form 3 — Inversion (Formal)

→ Flip the subject pronoun and the verb → connect with a hyphen

🧠 Critical note on sentence 5:
→ "Y a-t-il" — that -t- between "a" and "il" is a phonetic bridge
→ French avoids two vowel sounds colliding
→ Il a → A-t-il ? (the -t- is inserted for pronunciation only)
→ Il parle → Parle-t-il ? (same reason: "parle" ends in vowel sound, "il" starts with one)
→ Il finit → Finit-il ? (no -t- needed — "finit" already ends in "t")

🧠 QUI has TWO different behaviors:

🧠 QUE vs. QUOI — The rule:

PositionUseExample
Start of sentence (before est-ce que)Qu'est-ce queQu'est-ce que tu fais ?
Start of sentence (before inverted verb)QueQue fais-tu ?
End of sentence (casual)QuoiTu fais quoi ?
Standalone (alone, informal)Quoi ?Quoi ? (= Huh? What?)

→ Quoi = casual / end of sentence / standalone
→ Que = formal / start of sentence before inversion
→ Qu'est-ce que = standard / start of sentence in neutral register
→ They ALL mean "what" — the difference is register and position

🧠 Social note on sentence 78:
→ Saying "Quoi ?" alone is considered rude in polite company
→ Better alternative: "Comment ?" or "Pardon ?" (sentence 79)
→ Among friends → "Quoi ?" is totally fine

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Default to Form 2 (Est-ce que) in the beginning — it's always correct, never rude, never too formal
→ Learn Form 1 (Intonation) for friends and casual situations — it's the most natural in spoken French
→ Recognize Form 3 (Inversion) when you hear or read it — but don't force yourself to produce it daily
→ Memorize the fixed expressions: C'est combien ? / C'est quoi ? / C'est qui ? / C'est où ? — these are the survival questions
→ Match quel to the noun's gender — always check: is the noun masculine or feminine?
→ Connect to Project 3: questions often contain articles → "Où est la pharmacie ?" / "Il y a du café ?"

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT use "Quoi ?" alone in polite or formal settings → use "Comment ?" or "Pardon ?"
→ ❌ Do NOT mix up que and quel → "Que est ton nom ?" ❌ → "Quel est ton nom ?" ✅
→ ❌ Do NOT put quoi at the start of a standard sentence → "Quoi tu fais ?" ❌ → "Qu'est-ce que tu fais ?" ✅ or "Tu fais quoi ?" ✅
→ ❌ Do NOT use inversion in casual conversation with friends → it sounds unnatural and stiff
→ ❌ Do NOT forget the -t- bridge in inversion → "A-il ?" ❌ → "A-t-il ?" ✅
→ ❌ Do NOT say "Qu'est-ce que c'est ça ?" (mixing two structures) → either "C'est quoi, ça ?" OR "Qu'est-ce que c'est ?"
→ ❌ Do NOT use "quel" without a noun reference → "Quel tu fais ?" makes no sense

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Do NOT use inversion when speaking casually → you'll sound like a textbook from 1950
→ Do NOT use pourquoi at the end of sentences → unlike other question words, it strongly prefers the start
→ Do NOT use quel when you're asking about an action → quel is for NOUNS only
→ Do NOT use Form 3 (inversion) with "je" in most cases → "Parlé-je ?" sounds archaic
→ → Exception: "Puis-je ?" (May I?) — this specific inversion with "je" is still used formally
→ Do NOT answer pourquoi with pourquoi → answer with parce que (because)
→ Do NOT confuse comment (how) with quoi (what) → "Comment ?" alone = "What did you say?" (polite) — but "Comment tu fais ?" = "How do you do it?"

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

Project 1 → You exist and possess.
Project 2 → You act and describe your routine.
Project 3 → You point at and identify the world.
Project 4 → You ASK about the world — requesting any information you need.

The 3-form system is your social GPS:
→ Intonation = casual / spoken
→ Est-ce que = safe / neutral / always correct
→ Inversion = formal / written / professional

The 8 question words cover every possible type of information:
→ Person, thing, place, time, method, reason, quantity, specification

You can now have basic two-way interactions:
→ You state things (Projects 1-3) AND ask things (Project 4)

But there's a gap:
→ Someone says something wrong about you → you need to DENY it
→ Someone offers you something you don't want → you need to REFUSE
→ You can only say "yes" to questions — you cannot yet say "no" properly
