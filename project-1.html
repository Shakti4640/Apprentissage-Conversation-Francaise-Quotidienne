🟢 PROJECT 1: "I Exist, I Have Things"

You just landed in France — how do you state who you are and what you possess?

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You just stepped off a plane at Charles de Gaulle Airport, Paris
→ You have no French friends yet
→ You must check into a hotel, meet people, fill out forms, introduce yourself
→ You need to tell people WHO you are, WHAT you are, WHERE you are from, HOW you feel, and WHAT you have
→ You cannot describe actions yet — you can only state existence and possession
→ Every single interaction requires one of two engines: ÊTRE (to be) or AVOIR (to have)

The core realization:
→ In English, you say "I am hungry" — in French, you say "I have hunger"
→ In English, you say "I am 25 years old" — in French, you say "I have 25 years"
→ If you don't know which engine (être vs. avoir) controls which idea — every sentence you build will collapse

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of French sentences like a machine with an engine
→ Every basic French sentence has this skeleton:

SUBJECT → ENGINE (verb) → INFORMATION

→ At this stage, you only have two engines:

EngineFrenchPurpose
Engine 1ÊTREIdentity, state, location, description, profession, nationality, emotion-state
Engine 2AVOIRPossession, age, physical sensations, needs

→ Your job in Project 1: Learn which engine powers which type of sentence

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Read each sentence aloud — even if pronunciation is imperfect
→ Notice the pattern — not the individual words
→ Group by engine — ask yourself: "Is this an ÊTRE situation or an AVOIR situation?"
→ Memorize the sensation/age/idiom list — these are the ones that TRICK English speakers
→ Think in structures — "Subject + Engine + Information" is your skeleton forever

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT translate word-by-word from English
→ ❌ Do NOT say "Je suis faim" (I am hunger) — it's "J'ai faim"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "Je suis vingt ans" — it's "J'ai vingt ans"
→ ❌ Do NOT say "Je suis un médecin" — it's "Je suis médecin" (no article)
→ ❌ Do NOT skip gender agreement on adjectives — "fatiguée" if female, "fatigué" if male
→ ❌ Do NOT use "nous" in casual speech — use "on" (this is how real French people talk)

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ ÊTRE is NOT for physical sensations (hunger, thirst, cold, heat, sleep, pain)
→ AVOIR is NOT for descriptions (tall, kind, beautiful, ready, sick)
→ ÊTRE is NOT for age — ever
→ AVOIR is NOT for location — ever
→ If you're confused → ask: "In English, does this use 'am/is/are' but describe something you FEEL PHYSICALLY?"
→ If yes → probably AVOIR in French

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

Every French sentence starts with a SUBJECT and an ENGINE.
At this level, your only two engines are ÊTRE and AVOIR.
Mastering which engine powers which meaning is the single most important foundation in French.
If this is wrong — every future tense, every complex structure, every advanced sentence will be built on a broken foundation.

→ This project gave you static states — who you are, what you have, how you feel
→ But life is not static — you DO things every day
→ That requires new engines — action verbs in the present tense
