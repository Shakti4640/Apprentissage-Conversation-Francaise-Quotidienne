🟡 PROJECT 28: "Because, Therefore, Since"

You need to explain reasons and consequences logically — the backbone of adult conversation

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're in a meeting at work — your French boss asks: "Pourquoi le projet est-il en retard ?"
→ You know the reason — but you can only say:

❌ "Le projet est en retard. Le fournisseur n'a pas livré à temps."

→ Two disconnected sentences — a child's explanation
→ A French colleague says:

✅ "Le projet est en retard PARCE QUE le fournisseur n'a pas livré à temps."
✅ "COMME le fournisseur n'a pas livré à temps, le projet a pris du retard."
✅ "Le fournisseur n'a pas livré à temps, C'EST POURQUOI le projet est en retard."
✅ "ÉTANT DONNÉ QUE le fournisseur a eu des problèmes, nous avons dû repousser la date."

→ Same information — but four different ways to connect cause and consequence
→ Each connector sits in a different position, carries a different register, and implies a different logic

→ At dinner, your friend asks why you look tired:

❌ "Je suis fatigué. J'ai travaillé tard hier soir."

→ Disconnected again — a French person would say:

✅ "Je suis fatigué PARCE QUE j'ai travaillé tard hier soir."
✅ "J'ai travaillé tard hier soir, DONC je suis fatigué."
✅ "J'ai travaillé tard, DU COUP je suis crevé."
✅ "COMME j'ai travaillé tard, je suis fatigué."

→ You realize: adult French conversation is built on logical connections
→ Every explanation, every argument, every justification requires a cause-consequence connector
→ Without them, you list facts — with them, you reason, explain, and persuade

The core realization:
→ Projects 22-27 gave you tools to express opinions, emotions, obligations, and probability
→ But these were isolated attitudes — single-sentence expressions
→ Project 28 teaches you to LINK ideas — to say WHY something happens and WHAT FOLLOWS
→ This is the logic engine of French — the system that transforms isolated thoughts into coherent reasoning

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Every cause-consequence sentence has two parts:

CAUSE (the reason) ←→ CONSEQUENCE (the result)

→ French connectors fall into two families:

FamilyDirectionQuestion AnsweredExamples
CAUSE connectors← Why did this happen?Pourquoi ?parce que, car, puisque, comme, étant donné que
CONSEQUENCE connectors→ What happened as a result?Et alors ?donc, alors, c'est pourquoi, par conséquent, du coup

→ Some connectors introduce the CAUSE (reason-first)
→ Some connectors introduce the CONSEQUENCE (result-first)
→ Some can go in different positions within the sentence
→ Each one has a specific register (casual / neutral / formal) and logic (new info / known info / obvious)

4Il a réussi **parce qu'**il a beaucoup travaillé.He succeeded because he worked a lot.Achievement explanation
5Je ne suis pas venu parce que j'étais malade.I didn't come because I was sick.Past excuse
6Nous avons déménagé parce que l'appartement était trop petit.We moved because the apartment was too small.Life decision
7Elle a choisi Lyon parce que c'est plus calme.She chose Lyon because it's quieter.Decision reasoning

🧠 "PARCE QUE" — Core Rules:

POSITION: Always AFTER the consequence (or as a standalone answer)
→ "Je suis fatigué PARCE QUE j'ai mal dormi."
→ Consequence first → PARCE QUE → Cause

LOGIC: Introduces a NEW reason — the listener doesn't know it yet
→ Used to ANSWER the question "pourquoi?"
→ The most NEUTRAL and UNIVERSAL cause connector

CONTRACTION: Parce que → parce qu' before vowel
→ parce qu'il, parce qu'elle, parce qu'on, parce qu'un

REGISTER: Works in ALL contexts — casual, formal, written, spoken

SENTENCE 9 — SPECIAL STRUCTURE:
→ "Ce n'est pas parce que X que Y"
→ = "Just because X doesn't mean Y"
→ Very common argumentative structure
→ "Ce n'est pas parce qu'il pleut qu'on ne peut pas sortir."

Scenario: Written French, formal explanations, literary style

#French SentenceEnglishContext

12Je n'ai pas pu venir, car j'avais un empêchement.I couldn't come, for I had a prior commitment.Formal excuse

15Il ne viendra pas, car il est souffrant.He won't come, for he is unwell.Formal announcement

🧠 "CAR" vs. "PARCE QUE" — The Critical Difference:

PARCE QUE = Answers "pourquoi?" — gives a NEW reason
→ "Pourquoi tu es en retard ?" — "Parce que le bus..."
→ Can be used ALONE as an answer
→ Used in ALL registers

CAR = Does NOT answer "pourquoi?" — JUSTIFIES a previous statement
→ "Il faut agir vite, car le temps presse."
→ Cannot be used alone as an answer: "Pourquoi ?" — "Car..." ❌
→ Cannot start a sentence: "Car il pleut, je reste." ❌
→ Always preceded by a COMMA
→ Mainly WRITTEN / FORMAL French

KEY TEST:
→ Can you answer "Pourquoi?" with it? → PARCE QUE ✅ / CAR ❌
→ Is it adding a justification to something already stated? → CAR ✅

🧠 "PUISQUE" vs. "PARCE QUE" — The Logic Difference:

PARCE QUE = The listener DOESN'T KNOW the reason → you're INFORMING
→ "Je suis en retard PARCE QUE le bus n'est pas passé."
→ The listener asked "pourquoi?" → you give new information

PUISQUE = The listener ALREADY KNOWS the reason → you're CONCLUDING
→ "PUISQUE tu es là, on peut commencer."
→ Both speakers SEE that you're here → drawing a conclusion from known fact

PUISQUE often carries:
→ A sense of OBVIOUS logic: "Well, since X is clearly true..."
→ Sometimes FRUSTRATION: "Puisque c'est comme ça, je m'en vais !"
→ Sometimes REPROACH: "Puisque tu n'as rien à faire, aide-moi !"

POSITION:
→ PUISQUE can go at the BEGINNING or MIDDLE of the sentence
→ "Puisque tu es là, on commence." (beginning)
→ "On commence puisque tu es là." (middle — less common but possible)
→ PARCE QUE almost always goes AFTER the consequence

28Comme c'était son anniversaire, on lui a fait une surprise.Since it was his birthday, we threw him a surprise.Past narrative
29Comme tu es le plus grand, tu porteras le sac.Since you're the tallest, you'll carry the bag.Logical assignment
30Comme il était tard, nous avons décidé de partir.As it was late, we decided to leave.Past narrative
31Comme elle parle trois langues, elle a trouvé un travail facilement.Since she speaks three languages, she found a job easily.Causal explanation
32Comme je te l'ai dit, la réunion est reportée.As I told you, the meeting is postponed.Reminder
33Comme il n'y avait plus de places, on a dû attendre le prochain train.Since there were no more seats, we had to wait for the next train.Past cause-effect
34Comme tu le sais, notre budget est limité.As you know, our budget is limited.Professional reminder

🧠 "COMME" (cause) — Core Rules:

POSITION: ONLY at the BEGINNING of the sentence
→ ✅ "Comme il pleuvait, on est restés."
→ ❌ "On est restés comme il pleuvait." ← WRONG for cause
   (This would mean "in the same way as" — comparison, not cause)

LOGIC: Sets the SCENE — cause first, then consequence
→ Very natural for STORYTELLING and PAST NARRATIVES
→ "Comme il faisait beau, on est allés à la plage."
→ The "comme" clause sets up the situation → then the action follows

REGISTER: Universal — works in casual and formal contexts

TENSE PREFERENCE: Often used with IMPARFAIT (background setting)
→ Connection to Projects 10-11: Imparfait for background in narratives
→ "Comme il PLEUVAIT..." (imparfait = background)
→ "...on EST RESTÉS." (passé composé = action)

DO NOT CONFUSE:
→ COMME (cause) = Since/As — at the BEGINNING
→ COMME (comparison) = Like/As — in the MIDDLE
   "Il est grand comme son père." = He's tall like his father. (comparison)
→ Position determines meaning

🧠 THE FORMAL CAUSE CONNECTOR FAMILY:

ÉTANT DONNÉ QUE + clause = Given that... (formal)
ÉTANT DONNÉ + NOUN       = Given [noun]... (formal — no verb needed)
→ "Étant donné QUE les résultats sont bons..." (with clause)
→ "Étant donné les résultats..." (with noun only — shorter)

VU QUE + clause = Given that / Seeing that... (neutral-casual)
VU + NOUN       = Given [noun]... (neutral — no verb needed)
→ "Vu QUE tu es là..." (with clause)
→ "Vu les circonstances..." (with noun only)

DU FAIT QUE + clause = Due to the fact that... (very formal)
DU FAIT DE + NOUN    = Due to [noun]... (very formal)
→ "Du fait QUE les prix augmentent..." (with clause)
→ "Du fait DE l'inflation..." (with noun only)

REGISTER LADDER:
💨 Vu que         = casual-neutral (spoken)
🌤️ Étant donné que = formal (written/professional)
⚡ Du fait que     = very formal (legal/administrative)

🧠 THE NOUN-CAUSE TRIO — CRITICAL DISTINCTION:

GRÂCE À + noun = Thanks to — POSITIVE cause
→ The cause is GOOD — something HELPED
→ "Grâce à toi, j'ai réussi." (You helped → I succeeded)
→ NEVER use for negative outcomes:
   ❌ "Grâce à la pluie, le match a été annulé." (WRONG — rain is negative here)

À CAUSE DE + noun = Because of — NEGATIVE cause
→ The cause is BAD — something HARMED or HINDERED
→ "À cause de toi, on est en retard." (Your fault → we're late)
→ NEVER use for positive outcomes:
   ❌ "À cause de ton aide, j'ai réussi." (WRONG — help is positive)

EN RAISON DE + noun = Due to — NEUTRAL cause (formal)
→ No judgment — just stating the factual cause
→ "En raison de travaux, la route est fermée." (Neutral — administrative)
→ Used in official announcements, signs, formal writing
→ Can replace either grâce à or à cause de in formal contexts

CONTRACTION RULES:
→ grâce à + le = grâce AU
→ grâce à + les = grâce AUX
→ à cause de + le = à cause DU
→ à cause de + les = à cause DES
→ en raison de + le = en raison DU
→ en raison de + les = en raison DES

🧠 THE CONSEQUENCE TRIO — CRITICAL DISTINCTIONS:

DONC = So / Therefore — LOGICAL conclusion
→ Position: after the cause clause (or between subject and verb in formal: "Il est donc parti")
→ Register: UNIVERSAL — works everywhere
→ Logic: Strict logical deduction — A therefore B
→ "Il pleut, DONC je prends mon parapluie."
→ Can also go MID-SENTENCE in formal style:
   "Il est DONC impossible de continuer." (formal)
→ The most NEUTRAL consequence connector

ALORS = So / Then — NARRATIVE consequence
→ Position: after the cause clause
→ Register: CASUAL — mainly spoken French
→ Logic: "This happened, and THEN as a result..."
→ More STORYTELLING — less strict logic
→ "Il n'y avait plus de train, ALORS j'ai pris un taxi."
→ Also used as "well then" / "so" (conversation filler):
   "Alors, qu'est-ce qu'on fait ?" = "So, what do we do?"
→ Connection to Project 43: "Alors" as discourse marker

DU COUP = So / As a result — SPOKEN consequence
→ Position: after the cause clause
→ Register: VERY CASUAL — spoken French ONLY
→ Logic: Direct, immediate consequence — almost "bang, result"
→ "Mon réveil n'a pas sonné, DU COUP je suis en retard."
→ EXTREMELY common in daily spoken French
→ Connection to Project 43: "Du coup" as discourse marker
→ ⚠️ Avoid in formal writing — considered too casual
→ Some French purists criticize overuse of "du coup" — but everyone uses it

🧠 POSITION OF "DONC" — Flexible:

AFTER CAUSE (most common):
→ "Il pleut, DONC je reste."

MID-SENTENCE (formal):
→ "Il est DONC impossible de..."
→ "Nous avons DONC décidé de..."
→ "C'est DONC lui qui a raison."
→ More elegant in formal writing — sounds literary

BEGINNING OF SENTENCE:
→ "DONC, tu viens ou pas ?" (casual — almost = "So,...")
→ This is more of a conversation marker than a strict connector

🧠 THE "SO MUCH THAT" SYSTEM:

TELLEMENT + ADJECTIVE + QUE = So [adjective] that...
→ "Il est tellement fatigué qu'il dort." (He's so tired that he sleeps)

TELLEMENT + VERB + QUE = [Verb] so much that...
→ "Il a tellement mangé qu'il a eu mal." (He ate so much that he felt sick)
→ Position: tellement goes AFTER the verb (or between auxiliary and PP)

TELLEMENT DE + NOUN + QUE = So many/much [noun] that...
→ "Il y a tellement de monde qu'on ne peut pas entrer."

SI + ADJECTIVE/ADVERB + QUE = So [adj/adv] that... (slightly more formal)
→ "Il fait si froid qu'on ne sort pas."
→ SI is a more elegant alternative to TELLEMENT for adjectives/adverbs

TANT + VERB + QUE = So much that... (formal/literary — equivalent to tellement)
→ "Il a tant travaillé qu'il est tombé malade."

TANT DE + NOUN + QUE = So many/much that... (formal)
→ "J'ai tant de travail que je ne dors plus."

REGISTER:
→ TELLEMENT = universal (casual + formal)
→ SI = slightly formal (more elegant)
→ TANT = formal / literary

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Master the Big 5 first: parce que, comme, donc, alors, du coup — these cover 90% of daily needs
→ Always match cause connector to logic:
→ New reason → parce que
→ Known/obvious reason → puisque
→ Scene-setting → comme
→ Always match consequence connector to register:
→ Casual → du coup / alors / c'est pour ça que
→ Neutral → donc / c'est pourquoi
→ Formal → par conséquent / de ce fait
→ Learn the noun-cause trio: grâce à (positive) / à cause de (negative) / en raison de (neutral-formal)
→ Practice linking your sentences — never leave two related facts disconnected
→ Read French articles — count the cause-consequence connectors — they appear in every paragraph

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT use "car" to answer "pourquoi?"

WrongCorrect

WrongCorrect

WrongCorrect

→ ❌ Do NOT use "à cause de" for positive causes

WrongCorrect

→ ❌ Do NOT use "du coup" in formal writing

Wrong RegisterCorrect

Wrong LogicCorrect

→ ❌ Do NOT use "en raison de" in casual conversation

Wrong RegisterCorrect

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

Cause and consequence connectors are the logic skeleton of adult French.

→ Without them, you list facts like a child
→ With them, you reason, explain, justify, and argue like an adult

→ Two families, one goal:

FamilyDirectionKey Connectors
CAUSE (←why?)Explaining the reasonparce que, car, puisque, comme, grâce à, à cause de
CONSEQUENCE (→result)Stating the outcomedonc, alors, du coup, c'est pourquoi, par conséquent

→ Three critical distinctions to never forget:

DistinctionRule
parce que vs. puisqueNew reason vs. known/obvious reason
grâce à vs. à cause dePositive cause vs. negative cause
donc vs. du coupUniversal/logical vs. very casual/spoken

→ But what about when you want to express the OPPOSITE logic?
→ "He's rich, BUT he's not happy" — "Even though it's raining, we went out"
→ That's concession and opposition — the other side of logical thinking
