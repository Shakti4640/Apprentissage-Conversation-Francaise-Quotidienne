🟡 PROJECT 20: "The Two Magic Words: Y and En"

You keep saying the full place or quantity — natives don't

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're living in France — your French is getting better every week
→ You use COD/COI pronouns from Project 19 — people and things get replaced
→ But you still sound heavy in two specific situations:

Situation 1 — Places:

❌ Tu vas à Paris ? — Oui, je vais à Paris demain.
❌ Tu es allé au supermarché ? — Oui, je suis allé au supermarché.

→ A French person would say:

✅ Tu vas à Paris ? — Oui, j'y vais demain.
✅ Tu es allé au supermarché ? — Oui, j'y suis allé.

Situation 2 — Quantities and "de + noun":

❌ Tu veux du café ? — Oui, je veux du café.
❌ Tu as des enfants ? — Oui, j'ai trois enfants.
❌ Tu reviens de Paris ? — Oui, je reviens de Paris.

→ A French person would say:

✅ Tu veux du café ? — Oui, j'en veux.
✅ Tu as des enfants ? — Oui, j'en ai trois.
✅ Tu reviens de Paris ? — Oui, j'en reviens.

→ Y and En are the two most frequently used compression words in spoken French
→ You hear them in every single conversation — dozens of times per minute
→ Without them, you sound like a foreigner reading from a textbook
→ With them, you sound like someone who thinks in French

The core realization:
→ Project 19 gave you pronouns for people and things (le, la, les, lui, leur)
→ Project 20 gives you pronouns for places, abstract "à" references, quantities, and "de" references
→ Together, these cover every possible noun replacement in French
→ After this project, you will never need to repeat a noun again

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of Y and En as two vacuum cleaners — each one sucks up a specific type of phrase:

PronounSucks UpReplaces

Yà + thing/ideaJe pense à mon travail → J'y pense

→ Position rule: SAME as COD/COI (Project 19) — before the conjugated verb or before the infinitif

🔧 HOW CAN WE SOLVE IT

THE Y SYSTEM — Complete Map

What Y Replaces:

1. à + PLACE       → J'habite à Lyon → J'y habite
2. à + THING/IDEA  → Je pense à ça → J'y pense
3. Location preps   → Je suis dans le jardin → J'y suis
   (dans, sur, en, chez, sous, devant, derrière)

What Y Does NOT Replace:

❌ à + PERSON → Do NOT use Y for people
   Je pense à Marie → Je pense à ELLE (stress pronoun — NOT j'y pense)
   Exception: some speakers DO say "j'y pense" for people in casual speech
   but the formal rule says NO

THE EN SYSTEM — Complete Map

What En Replaces:

1. de + NOUN              → Je parle de mon travail → J'en parle
2. du / de la / de l' / des (partitive)  → Je mange du pain → J'en mange
3. QUANTITY + noun         → J'ai deux frères → J'en ai deux
4. un / une + noun         → J'ai une voiture → J'en ai une
5. de + PLACE (origin)     → Je viens de Paris → J'en viens
6. expressions with "de"   → J'ai besoin de repos → J'en ai besoin

What En Does NOT Replace:

❌ de + SPECIFIC PERSON → Do NOT use En for specific people
   Je parle de Marie → Je parle d'ELLE (stress pronoun — NOT j'en parle)
   But: Je parle de mes problèmes → J'en parle ✅ (things = OK)

🧠 CRITICAL RULE — QUANTITY STAYS BEHIND:

When EN replaces a noun with a quantity:
→ The NOUN disappears
→ The QUANTITY WORD remains at the end

J'ai trois CHATS → J'en ai trois.
        ↑ gone      ↑ stays

J'ai beaucoup de TRAVAIL → J'en ai beaucoup.
              ↑ gone         ↑ stays

→ This is unique to en — no other pronoun works this way

🧠 Y vs. En — The Direction Split:

GOING TO a place     → Y    (Je vais à Paris → J'y vais)
COMING FROM a place  → EN   (Je viens de Paris → J'en viens)

Y  = DESTINATION  (→ toward)
EN = ORIGIN       (← from)

→ This is one of the cleanest patterns in French — destination = Y, origin = En

🧠 CRITICAL RULE — NO AGREEMENT WITH EN + AVOIR:

Normal COD (Project 19):
→ Les pommes ? Je LES ai achetéES. (agreement with "les" = fem plural)

With EN:
→ Des pommes ? J'EN ai acheté. (NO agreement — en blocks it)
→ J'en ai acheté trois. (still no agreement)

WHY? → En is considered "indefinite" — it doesn't carry gender/number clearly
→ Therefore the past participle stays UNCHANGED
→ This is the OPPOSITE of COD pronouns from Project 19

→ Exception alert: Some strict grammarians argue for agreement with en — but in practice and in modern French, no agreement is standard

🧠 CRITICAL SPELLING RULES FOR IMPÉRATIF + Y/EN:

1. "Va" normally has NO -s → but before Y:
   → Va ! (normal) → Vas-y ! (add -s for pronunciation flow)

2. "Mange" normally has NO -s → but before en:
   → Mange ! (normal) → Manges-en ! (some sources) OR Mange-en ! (debated)
   → In practice, the -s is added for SOUND — "vas-y" is universal

3. Negative impératif → Y/En jump BACK before the verb:
   → AFFIRMATIVE: Vas-y ! Prends-en !
   → NEGATIVE:    N'y va pas ! N'en prends pas !
   → Same pattern as COD/COI from Project 19

🧠 "VAS-Y" and "ALLONS-Y" — The Most Common Expressions in Spoken French:
→ Vas-y ! = Go ahead / Do it / Come on / Let's do this
→ Allons-y ! = Let's go / Let's start
→ These are used hundreds of times daily — far beyond their literal meaning of "go there"

🌉 BRIDGE TO PROJECT 21

You can now compress nouns into pronouns — your sentences are lighter
But you still describe things in separate sentences:

→ "J'ai un ami. Cet ami habite à Paris. Cet ami parle trois langues."

A French person would combine all of this into one sentence:
→ "J'ai un ami qui habite à Paris et qui parle trois langues."

→ Qui, que, où, dont — these are relative pronouns
→ They let you build complex sentences by connecting ideas within a single structure
→ This is the difference between speaking like a child and speaking like an adult
