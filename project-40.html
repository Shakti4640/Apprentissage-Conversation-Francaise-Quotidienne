🟠 PROJECT 40: "Suit Up or Dress Down"

The same day lived twice — once as a corporate executive, once as a student

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You wake up on Monday morning in Lyon
→ Version A: You are a 45-year-old corporate executive — meetings with clients, lunch with the CEO, email to the board
→ Version B: You are a 22-year-old university student — texting friends, chatting in the cafeteria, joking at a party
→ Both versions of you speak French — but it's NOT the same French
→ The executive says: "Pourriez-vous m'accorder quelques instants ?"
→ The student says: "T'as deux secondes ?"
→ Same meaning. Completely different language.
→ You've spent 39 projects learning HOW to build French sentences
→ Project 40 teaches you how to ADJUST those sentences to match the social situation
→ You already touched on tu/vous (P1), formal future (P16), conditionnel for politeness (P17)
→ But register is not just one feature — it's a COMPLETE SYSTEM that affects:

  1. Pronouns (tu vs. vous / on vs. nous)
  2. Vocabulary (words change entirely)
  3. Sentence length (short and chopped vs. long and elaborate)
  4. Negation (NE drops in casual speech)
  5. Question forms (inversion vs. intonation)
  6. Verb tenses (passé simple in writing vs. passé composé in speech)
  7. Filler words (absent in formal, everywhere in casual)
  8. Pronunciation shortcuts (full pronunciation vs. swallowed sounds)

The core realization:
→ Grammar is the SKELETON — register is the CLOTHING
→ The same skeleton (subject + verb + object) gets dressed differently for every social context
→ Using formal French with friends makes you sound cold and distant
→ Using casual French with a boss makes you sound disrespectful and unprofessional
→ Mastering register = mastering social survival in France

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ Think of French as having four main registers — like gears in a car

RegisterNameUsed WithTone
🎩 R4Soutenu (Formal/Literary)Official documents, speeches, literature, very formal meetingsElegant, distant, impressive
👔 R3Courant (Standard)Professional emails, meetings, strangers, news, teachersCorrect, neutral, respectful
👕 R2Familier (Casual)Friends, family, close colleagues, everyday conversationRelaxed, warm, natural
🩳 R1Argotique (Slang/Very casual)Close friends, youth, texting, jokesRaw, playful, tribal

→ Most French courses teach ONLY R3 (standard)
→ Most French PEOPLE speak in R2 (casual) daily
→ French literature and official speech use R4 (formal)
→ French youth and social media use R1 (slang)
→ You need to RECOGNIZE all four and PRODUCE at least R3 and R2

🧠 Dimension unlocked: NOUS vs. ON

Register"We"Example

→ In modern spoken French, ON has almost completely replaced NOUS for "we"
→ NOUS sounds formal, stiff, or old-fashioned in casual conversation
→ ON is grammatically 3rd person singular → verb conjugation matches il/elle
→ BUT past participle can agree in plural when ON clearly means "we":

  • "On est arrivé" ✅ (also accepted — grammatically singular)

🧠 Dimension unlocked: THE POLITENESS GRADIENT

MOST POLITE ←——————————————————————————→ MOST DIRECT
Auriez-vous l'obligeance → Pourriez-vous → Vous pouvez → Tu peux → Ferme → La porte !
     R4 ultra-formal          R3 polite      R3 light     R2 casual  R2 direct  R1 raw

→ Four tools for increasing politeness (all from previous projects):

  1. Conditionnel (P17): "Pourriez-vous..." — softer than present
  2. Si-clause (P33): "Si tu pouvais..." — even softer (hypothetical)
  3. Vous (P1): adds formality distance
  4. Longer sentence = more polite (more words = more effort = more respect)

→ Four tools for increasing directness:

  1. Impératif (P13): "Ferme la porte"
  2. Tu: reduces distance
  3. Shorter sentence: fewer words = less ceremony
  4. Just the noun: "La porte !" = maximum compression

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Identify your DEFAULT register — most learners default to R3 (standard) because that's what textbooks teach
→ Actively practice R2 — this is how 90% of daily French conversation happens
→ Learn to RECOGNIZE R4 — you'll encounter it in formal letters, literature, official documents
→ Learn to RECOGNIZE R1 — you'll hear it in movies, music, among young people, on social media
→ Match your register to your interlocutor:

Speaking ToUse Register
Boss, client, stranger, elderly person👔 R3 (vous, full sentences, conditionnel)
Friend, family, close colleague👕 R2 (tu, shortened, on, NE dropped)
Very close friend, same-age peer🩳 R1 (slang, ultra-short, fillers)
Official letter, academic paper🎩 R4 (formulaic closings, passé simple recognition)

→ Practice the SAME sentence in multiple registers — this is the best exercise
→ Connect to ALL previous projects — register is not a new structure, it's a FILTER applied to everything

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ NEVER use R1 slang with someone you don't know well:

  • ❌ To a boss: "Wesh, j'peux pas venir demain" → sounds disrespectful
  • ✅ To a boss: "Je ne pourrai malheureusement pas être présent demain"

→ ❌ NEVER use R4 formal French with close friends:

  • ❌ To a friend: "Auriez-vous l'obligeance de me passer le sel ?" → sounds sarcastic
  • ✅ To a friend: "Tu me passes le sel ?"

→ ❌ NEVER use passé simple in speech:

  • ❌ Speaking: "Hier, j'allai au cinéma" → people will think you're quoting a novel
  • ✅ Speaking: "Hier, je suis allé au cinéma" (passé composé — always in speech)

→ ❌ NEVER drop NE in formal writing:

  • ❌ Email to client: "Je sais pas si c'est possible" → unprofessional
  • ✅ Email to client: "Je ne sais pas si cela est possible"

→ ❌ NEVER use "tu" with someone you've just met (unless they're clearly your age in a casual context):

  • When in doubt → use VOUS → wait for them to say "On peut se tutoyer" (We can use tu)
  • Switching from vous to tu is called "le tutoiement" — it's a social RITUAL in France

→ ❌ NEVER mix registers randomly in the same sentence:

  • ❌ "Pourriez-vous me passer le truc ?" (R3 structure + R1 vocabulary = jarring)
  • ✅ "Pourriez-vous me transmettre le document ?" (R3 + R3 = coherent)
  • ✅ "Tu me passes le truc ?" (R2 + R2 = coherent)

→ ❌ NEVER assume one register is "correct" and others are "wrong":

  • ALL registers are valid French — used in different contexts
  • "Bouffer" is not "bad French" — it's casual French
  • "Se restaurer" is not "better French" — it's formal French
  • The WRONG register in the WRONG context is the error — not the register itself
