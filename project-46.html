🔴 PROJECT 46: "What If What If What If"

A philosophical discussion where every hypothesis leads to another hypothesis

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ You're at a late-night philosophical discussion in a Parisian apartment
→ The wine is flowing, the conversation is deep, and someone asks:
→ "Si l'humanité n'avait jamais inventé l'écriture, est-ce que la civilisation existerait quand même ?"
→ Someone responds: "Peut-être, mais à supposer qu'elle existe, elle serait radicalement différente."
→ Another adds: "Quand bien même elle aurait existé, nous n'en saurions rien — par définition."
→ Someone pushes further: "Et si, en plus de ne pas avoir inventé l'écriture, nous n'avions pas non plus développé le langage ?"
→ Another layer: "Au cas où vous l'auriez oublié, sans langage, nous ne serions même pas en train d'avoir cette conversation."
→ You follow the LOGIC perfectly — but you can't BUILD these sentences yourself
→ You know P18 (real conditions), P33 (unreal present), P34 (unreal past)
→ But these people are doing something MORE — they're:

  1. Mixing time levels — past conditions with present results
  2. Chaining conditions — one hypothesis built on top of another
  3. Using alternative conditional structures — not just "si" but "quand bien même," "au cas où," "à supposer que"
  4. Building cascading consequences — if A, then B, and if B, then C, and if C, then...
  5. Stacking impossibilities — imagining layers of unreality

The core realization:
→ P18/P33/P34 gave you single-level si-clauses — one condition, one result
→ P46 gives you multi-level hypothetical architecture — chained, mixed, and alternative structures
→ This is where French hypothetical reasoning reaches its MAXIMUM complexity
→ It's used in: philosophy, literature, legal reasoning, strategic planning, intellectual debate, and deep conversation

🧠 Structure unlocked: Si + PLUS-QUE-PARFAIT → CONDITIONNEL PRÉSENT

→ The past action did not happen → the present reality would be different
→ The key word is often "aujourd'hui," "maintenant," "encore" — anchoring the result in the PRESENT
→ Compare with P34 (pure past): "Si j'avais étudié la médecine, j'aurais été médecin" (past result — both in the past)
→ Mixed: "Si j'avais étudié la médecine, je serais médecin" (past condition → present result — time levels CROSS)
→ How to tell the difference: Does the result apply NOW or THEN?

  • NOW → conditionnel présent (mixed)
  • THEN → conditionnel passé (pure P34)

🧠 Structure unlocked: Si + IMPARFAIT → CONDITIONNEL PASSÉ

→ The present condition is a permanent trait or state (brave, stubborn, rich, organized)
→ The past result would have been different IF that permanent trait were different
→ This is LESS common than Past→Present mix but very powerful for character analysis
→ Sentence 12: "Si tu étais vraiment mon ami" — the imparfait questions a PERMANENT relationship state → past consequence reveals the truth
→ Sentence 18: "Si il était honnête" — questioning someone's NATURE (present) to explain their past BEHAVIOR
→ Key insight: This mix is often used for reproach — "If you were [better quality], you would have [done the right thing]"

🧠 Structure unlocked: QUAND BIEN MÊME + conditionnel = Even if (emphatic)

QUAND BIEN MÊME vs. MÊME SI — What's the difference?

StructureMood After ItRegisterEmphasis Level
Même si + indicatif/imparfaitIndicatif or ImparfaitR2/R3Normal "even if"
Quand bien même + conditionnelConditionnel (always)R3/R4EMPHATIC "even if" — stronger, more dramatic

→ "Même si il s'excusait, je ne pardonnerais pas" = Even if he apologized (standard)
→ "Quand bien même il s'excuserait, je ne pardonnerais pas" = Even if he apologized (emphatic — stresses futility)

→ Never indicatif after quand bien même — this is what distinguishes it from "même si"

🧠 Structure unlocked: AU CAS OÙ + conditionnel = In case / Just in case

Key rules:
→ Always conditionnel after "au cas où" — NEVER indicatif, NEVER subjonctif
→ ✅ Au cas où il pleuvrait (conditionnel)
→ ❌ Au cas où il pleuve (subjonctif — wrong)
→ ❌ Au cas où il pleut (indicatif — wrong)

→ Can use conditionnel présent (for present/future possibilities) or conditionnel passé (for past possibilities)
→ Sentence 37: "Au cas où tu aurais oublié" — cond.passé — "in case you might have forgotten"
→ Sentence 33: "Au cas où vous ne le sauriez pas" — very polite way to inform someone — implies "you probably already know, but just in case..."

AU CAS OÙ vs. SI — When to use which:

StructureMeaningLikelihood

Au cas où il pleuvraitIn case it rainsPrecautionary — less likely but preparing

→ "Au cas où" is BETWEEN real "si" and unreal "si" — it's the precautionary zone

⚠️ The Expletive "NE" after À MOINS QUE — Sentence 62:

À moins que les fournisseurs NE soient en retard.
                              │
                     EXPLETIVE NE — not negation!

→ This "ne" is NOT negative — it doesn't mean "not"
→ It's a literary/formal leftover called "ne explétif"
→ It appears after: à moins que, avant que, de peur que, de crainte que
→ With it: "À moins qu'il ne vienne" = Unless he comes (formal ✅)
→ Without it: "À moins qu'il vienne" = Unless he comes (casual ✅)
→ Both are correct — the expletive "ne" is optional but more elegant
→ Do NOT add "pas" — that would make it truly negative and change the meaning entirely
→ ❌ "À moins qu'il ne vienne pas" = Unless he DOESN'T come (opposite meaning!)

🧠 Structure unlocked: CASCADING CONDITIONALS

The Cascade Pattern:

IF A hadn't happened → B wouldn't have happened
IF B hadn't happened → C wouldn't exist
IF C didn't exist → D would never have been possible

→ Each result becomes the CONDITION for the next link
→ The chain can MIX time levels — past conditions leading to present realities leading to future impossibilities
→ Sentence 69: Triple cascade in the same time level (P33 × 3) — "if X, then Y, then Z"
→ Sentence 70: Cascade using "sans" (without) as an alternative to "si" — equally valid and often more elegant

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ NEVER use indicatif after "au cas où":

  • ❌ Au cas où il pleut → ✅ Au cas où il pleuvrait
  • Always conditionnel after "au cas où"

→ ❌ NEVER use indicatif after "à supposer que" / "en admettant que":

  • ❌ À supposer que c'est vrai → ✅ À supposer que ce soit vrai
  • Always subjonctif after these structures

→ ❌ NEVER use conditionnel after "si" (same rule as P33/P34 — still applies):

  • ❌ Si j'aurais su → ✅ Si j'avais su
  • "Si" NEVER takes conditionnel — only imparfait or plus-que-parfait

→ ❌ NEVER confuse "quand bien même" with "même si":

  • "Même si" + indicatif/imparfait → standard "even if"
  • "Quand bien même" + conditionnel → emphatic "even if"
  • ❌ Quand bien même il pleut → ✅ Quand bien même il pleuvrait

→ ❌ NEVER add "pas" to expletive "ne" after "à moins que":

  • ✅ "À moins qu'il ne vienne" = Unless he comes (expletive ne — no negation)
  • ❌ "À moins qu'il ne vienne pas" = Unless he DOESN'T come (real negation — opposite meaning!)

→ ❌ NEVER chain more than 3-4 hypotheticals without pausing:

  • Long chains become impossible to follow
  • After 3 links → pause, summarize, then continue if needed
  • "En résumé, sans A, pas de B, et sans B, pas de C. Autrement dit..."

→ ❌ NEVER use literary inversions (Aurait-il su...) in casual speech:

  • ❌ Speaking to a friend: "Eût-il été là..." → they'll think you're joking
  • ✅ Speaking to a friend: "S'il avait été là..." → normal P34

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Mixed conditionals — only when the time levels genuinely CROSS

  • If both condition and result are in the past → use pure P34
  • If both are in the present → use pure P33
  • Mixed ONLY when past affects present or present explains past

→ Quand bien même — only in R3/R4

  • Too formal for casual chat → use "même si" instead
  • Perfect for debates, essays, intellectual discussion

→ Literary inversions (Aurait-il su...) — only in writing or very formal speech

  • In conversation → sounds pretentious or humorous
  • In literature/essays → sounds elegant and sophisticated

→ Long chains — only when the audience can follow

  • In writing → chains work because readers can re-read
  • In speech → maximum 2-3 links before summarizing

→ Expletive "ne" — only if you're comfortable with it

  • It's optional → omitting it is perfectly correct
  • Including it signals formal register mastery
  • Getting it WRONG (adding "pas") is worse than omitting it

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

P18/P33/P34 gave you three levels of si-clauses — real, unreal present, unreal past.
P46 gives you the COMPLETE hypothetical system — mixed levels, alternative structures, and cascading chains.

The five new tools:

  1. Mixed conditionals — crossing time levels (past↔present)
  2. Quand bien même + conditionnel — emphatic "even if"
  3. Au cas où + conditionnel — precautionary "in case"
  4. À supposer que / En admettant que + subjonctif — intellectual "assuming"
  5. Cascading chains — linking hypotheticals into domino sequences

The elegant alternatives to "si":

  • Sans + noun — replaces negative si-clauses
  • Avec + noun — replaces positive si-clauses
  • Inversion — literary replacement for si-clauses
  • Que + subjonctif — "whether...or not"

The mood rules are ABSOLUTE:

  • After si → NEVER conditionnel
  • After quand bien même → ALWAYS conditionnel
  • After au cas où → ALWAYS conditionnel
  • After à supposer que / à condition que / à moins que / pourvu que → ALWAYS subjonctif

You now have the DEEPEST level of French hypothetical reasoning — the ability to build, chain, mix, and explore any imagined reality.

But there's a gap between this sophisticated grammar and what French people ACTUALLY SAY every day. They drop sounds, contract words, skip rules, and speak a version of French that no textbook teaches.
