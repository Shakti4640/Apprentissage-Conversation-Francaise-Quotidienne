🔵 PROJECT 9: "What Happened Yesterday"

You need to tell a friend what you did last weekend

🎯 THE HYPOTHETICAL PROBLEM

→ It's Monday morning at your French language school in Paris
→ Your classmate Léa asks: "Alors, t'as fait quoi ce week-end ?" (So, what did you do this weekend?)
→ You had an amazing weekend — you visited Montmartre, ate at a great restaurant, watched a movie, met new people, walked along the Seine
→ You open your mouth and... you can only speak in present tense
→ "Je visite Montmartre. Je mange au restaurant. Je regarde un film."
→ Léa looks confused — are you describing your weekend or your plans for today?
→ You sound like a robot narrating in real time
→ You NEED to say:
→ → "I visited Montmartre."
→ → "I ate at a restaurant."
→ → "I watched a movie."
→ → "I met new people."
→ → "We walked along the Seine."
→ → "She left early."
→ → "We arrived late."
→ Every single one of these requires a new sentence structure — the passé composé

The core realization:
→ All of Phase 1 gave you the present tense — a flat, timeless world
→ The passé composé gives you a completed past — things that HAPPENED, that are DONE, that are FINISHED
→ It's the #1 past tense in spoken French — used far more than any other past form
→ It uses a completely new architecture: instead of one verb, you now build with TWO pieces
→ → Piece 1: An auxiliary verb (avoir OR être) — conjugated in present tense
→ → Piece 2: A past participle — the "done" form of the action verb
→ Which auxiliary you use (avoir or être) depends on the verb — and getting it wrong is one of the most common mistakes in French

🔍 HOW TO APPROACH IT

→ The passé composé sentence skeleton:

SUBJECT + AUXILIARY (avoir/être in present) + PAST PARTICIPLE + rest

→ Compare with present tense:

Present:         Je mange au restaurant.        (I eat at the restaurant.)
Passé composé:   J'ai mangé au restaurant.      (I ate at the restaurant.)

→ You already know avoir and être in present tense (Project 1)
→ You already know action verbs (Project 2)
→ Now you combine them in a new way

→ Two big questions to solve:

  1. How do you form the past participle of each verb?
  2. How do you know whether to use avoir or être as the auxiliary?

Step 2 — Choosing the Auxiliary: AVOIR or ÊTRE?

The Majority Rule: MOST verbs use AVOIR

J'AI mangé. Tu AS parlé. Il A travaillé. Nous AVONS regardé. Vous AVEZ écouté. Ils ONT fini.

→ ~95% of all French verbs use avoir in passé composé
→ If in doubt → try avoir → you'll be right most of the time

The Special Club: SOME verbs use ÊTRE

→ A small group of verbs — mostly describing movement or state change — use être instead
→ The classic mnemonic: DR & MRS VANDERTRAMP

→ *Passer uses être when it means "to pass by / to stop by" (no direct object)
→ Passer uses avoir when it means "to pass / to spend (time)" → J'ai passé une bonne journée

→ Also: ALL reflexive verbs (se lever, se laver — Project 14) use être
→ You'll learn this in Project 14 — for now, focus on the DR MRS VANDERTRAMP list

The CRITICAL difference when using être:

With AVOIR → past participle does NOT change

Il a mangé.    Elle a mangé.    Ils ont mangé.    Elles ont mangé.
(mangé stays the same — always)

With ÊTRE → past participle AGREES with the subject (like an adjective!)

Il est allé.        (he went)         — masculine singular
Elle est allée.     (she went)        — feminine singular → add -e
Ils sont allés.     (they went)       — masculine plural → add -s
Elles sont allées.  (they went, f)    — feminine plural → add -es
On est allés.       (we went)         — plural → add -s (common in spoken)

→ This agreement rule ONLY applies with être — never with avoir (at this level)
→ Think of it like this: with être, the past participle behaves like an adjective describing the subject
→ Recall from Project 1: "Elle est fatiguée" — same logic → "Elle est allée"

🧠 NEGATION PLACEMENT IN PASSÉ COMPOSÉ — THE RULE:

Subject + NE + AUXILIARY + PAS/PLUS/JAMAIS/RIEN + PAST PARTICIPLE + rest

→ The negation sandwich wraps around the AUXILIARY (avoir or être)
→ NOT around the past participle
→ The past participle comes AFTER the sandwich

PartPosition
NEBefore auxiliary
PAS/PLUS/JAMAIS/RIENAfter auxiliary, BEFORE participle
PAST PARTICIPLEAfter the negation word

→ ✅ Je **n'**ai pas mangé.
→ ❌ Je n'ai mangé pas.
→ ✅ Il **n'**a rien fait.
→ ❌ Il n'a fait rien.

🧠 Exception — PERSONNE:
→ Personne goes AFTER the past participle (it's "heavier"):
→ ✅ Je **n'**ai vu personne. (I didn't see anyone.)
→ Same behavior as with modals (Project 6, Block I)

🧠 Spoken French reminder (Project 5):
→ The "ne" drops in speech:
→ Written: Je **n'**ai pas mangé. → Spoken: J'ai pas mangé.
→ Written: Il **n'**a rien fait. → Spoken: Il a rien fait.

🧠 THE DUAL-AUXILIARY RULE:

→ Some verbs (monter, descendre, sortir, passer, rentrer, retourner) can use EITHER avoir or être
→ The rule:

Has a DIRECT OBJECT (doing something TO something)No direct object (just moving)
Use AVOIRUse ÊTRE
J'ai monté les valises.Je suis monté au 3e étage.
J'ai sorti le chien.Je suis sorti avec des amis.

→ When you DO something to an object → avoir (you're acting on something)
→ When you just MOVE → être (you're changing location)
→ At this level: don't panic — the être-only meaning (movement) is far more common in daily speech
→ Just be aware that the dual system exists

✅ WHAT SHOULD DO

→ Master the -ER participle pattern first (-er → -é) — it covers 90% of verbs
→ Memorize the top 15 irregular past participles (fait, pris, vu, bu, lu, dit, mis, écrit, eu, été, ouvert, connu, voulu, dû, pu)
→ Memorize the DR MRS VANDERTRAMP list — these are your ÊTRE verbs
→ Practice agreement with être — add -e for feminine, -s for plural, -es for feminine plural
→ Place negation around the AUXILIARY — ne + auxiliary + pas + participle
→ Connect to Project 1 — you already know avoir and être in present tense → same forms become your auxiliaries
→ Connect to Project 2 — the action verbs you learned become past participles: manger→mangé, finir→fini
→ Connect to Project 8 — past time markers (hier, la semaine dernière, il y a...) trigger passé composé

❌ WHAT SHOULD NOT DO

→ ❌ Do NOT use avoir with DR MRS VANDERTRAMP verbs → "J'ai allé" → "Je suis allé"
→ ❌ Do NOT use être with regular action verbs → "Je suis mangé" → "J'ai mangé"
→ ❌ Do NOT forget agreement with être → "Elle est allé" → "Elle est allée"
→ ❌ Do NOT add agreement with avoir (at this level) → "Elle a mangé" (NOT "mangée" in standard cases)
→ ❌ Do NOT put negation after the participle → "J'ai mangé pas" → "Je n'ai pas mangé"
→ ❌ Do NOT use present tense with past time markers → "Hier, je mange" → "Hier, j'ai mangé"
→ ❌ Do NOT conjugate the past participle like a verb → "J'ai mangés" → the participle doesn't conjugate for person, only for agreement with être
→ ❌ Do NOT confuse "il y a" (existence) with "il y a" (ago) → context tells you which

When to use PASSÉ COMPOSÉ (vs. other tenses):

Use Passé Composé When...Example
A specific action happened and is finishedJ'ai mangé à midi. (I ate at noon — done.)
An event occurred at a specific timeHier, je suis allé au cinéma.
Something happened a specific number of timesJ'ai visité Paris trois fois. (I visited Paris three times.)
A sequence of events occurredJ'ai mangé, j'ai payé, je suis parti. (I ate, paid, left.)
You want to express "ago"Il y a deux jours, j'ai perdu mon téléphone.

🚫 WHEN NOT TO USE

→ Do NOT use passé composé for descriptions of how things WERE → that's imparfait (Project 10)
→ → "The weather was nice" ≠ passé composé → imparfait
→ → "I was tired" ≠ passé composé → imparfait
→ Do NOT use passé composé for habitual past actions → that's imparfait (Project 10)
→ → "I used to eat there every day" ≠ passé composé → imparfait
→ Do NOT use passé composé for ongoing background actions → that's imparfait (Project 10)
→ → "While I was walking..." ≠ passé composé → imparfait
→ Do NOT use passé composé for things happening RIGHT NOW → that's present tense
→ Do NOT use passé composé for future plans → that's futur proche (Project 12) or futur simple (Project 16)

🔑 KEY TAKEAWAY

Phase 1 (Projects 1-8) → You mastered the PRESENT — existence, action, space, time.
Project 9 → You unlocked the COMPLETED PAST — things that happened and are done.

The passé composé architecture:

Subject + AUXILIARY (avoir/être) + PAST PARTICIPLE + rest

Three critical rules:

  1. ~95% of verbs use AVOIR → the rest (DR MRS VANDERTRAMP) use ÊTRE
  2. With être → past participle agrees with subject (like an adjective)
  3. Negation wraps around the auxiliary → ne + auxiliary + pas + participle

You can now tell stories about what HAPPENED.
But your stories are flat — they're just lists of events:
→ "I went. I ate. I saw. I left."
→ There's no atmosphere, no background, no "how things were"

Real stories need TWO layers:
→ Layer 1: What HAPPENED (events) → Passé composé ✅ (this project)
→ Layer 2: How things WERE (background, descriptions, feelings, habits) → Imparfait (next project)

Without the imparfait, your French stories sound like police reports.
With it, they sound like real human narratives.
